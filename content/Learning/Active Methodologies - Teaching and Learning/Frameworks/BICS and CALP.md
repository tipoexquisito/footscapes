A distinction developed by Jim Cummins in the 1970s and 1980s to describe two different dimensions of language proficiency.

# BICS
**BICS — Basic Interpersonal Communicative Skills**
The language used in everyday social interaction: conversation, informal exchange, social contexts. BICS develops relatively quickly — typically within two to three years of exposure. It is context-embedded and cognitively undemanding.

# CALP
**CALP — Cognitive Academic Language Proficiency**
The language required for academic tasks: reading complex texts, constructing arguments, understanding subject-specific terminology, writing analytically. CALP takes significantly longer to develop — five to seven years or more — and is context-reduced and cognitively demanding.

The distinction matters in practice because learners who appear fluent in social interaction may still struggle with academic language. Apparent fluency can mask real gaps. This is relevant for language teachers, CLIL practitioners, and educators working with multilingual learners.
