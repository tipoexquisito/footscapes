> "AI literacy represents the technical knowledge, durable skills, and future-ready attitudes required to thrive in a world influenced by AI."
> AI Literacy Framework (2025) — [ailiteracyframework.org](https://ailiteracyframework.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/AILitFramework_ReviewDraft.pdf)

AI literacy enables learners to engage, create with, manage, and design AI — while critically evaluating its benefits, risks, and ethical implications. It is not about tool use. It is about understanding systems, questioning outputs, and maintaining human agency.

---

## Three Dimensions

**Technical knowledge** — understanding how AI systems work: data, models, training, outputs, limitations.

**Durable skills** — critical thinking, communication, collaboration, creativity. The same skills that transfer across domains and resist automation.

**Future-ready attitudes** — ethical awareness, healthy scepticism, openness to change. Knowing when to trust, when to question, and when to refuse.

> [!concept] Not tool use — agency
> AI literacy is not about knowing which tools exist. It is about maintaining human agency in relation to systems that are designed to shape perception, generate content, and simulate authority. The literate person asks: who built this, on what data, toward what end, and who benefits?

## The Deeper Question

The session built around a provocation: *What is truth in 2026?*

AI literacy, in this context, is inseparable from [[Media Literacy]] and Critical Thinking. The capacity to evaluate AI outputs is the same capacity needed to evaluate any information system — and it begins with understanding how knowledge is constructed, distributed, and shaped.

> [!quote] The provocation
> "What is true? Who decides? And how do you know?"
> The question was not rhetorical. It was the lesson.
