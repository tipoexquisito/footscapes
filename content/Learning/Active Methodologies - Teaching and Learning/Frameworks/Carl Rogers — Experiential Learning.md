Carl Rogers (1902–1987), American psychologist and founder of person-centred therapy, proposed that meaningful learning is inseparable from personal experience and emotional engagement.

---

## The Core Argument

Learning that has no connection to lived experience does not stay. What is relevant to the learner — connected to their needs, questions, and sense of self — is retained. What is merely transmitted is forgotten.

Rogers distinguished between cognitive learning (information acquisition) and experiential learning (personal change and growth). The second is qualitatively different: it involves the whole person, not only the intellect.

> "We feel, therefore we learn."

---

## The Facilitator, Not the Expert

The teacher's role shifts accordingly. The facilitator does not deliver knowledge — they create the conditions under which learning becomes possible: a climate of trust, openness to error, and genuine curiosity.

This connects directly to [[Active Methodologies]] and the principle that learner agency is not a technique but a relational condition.
