A cooperative learning technique that develops negotiation, active listening, and collective decision-making. Groups work toward a shared position on a complex issue through structured role-based discussion.

---

## The Task

Groups receive a scenario involving competing interests and limited resources — for example, a school facing budget cuts must decide which programmes to protect and which to reduce.

Each participant takes a role with a specific interest to represent: the headteacher prioritising institutional stability, the teacher prioritising pedagogical continuity, the parent prioritising specific services, the student prioritising daily experience, the local authority prioritising compliance and cost.

The group must negotiate toward a single shared proposal that all members are willing to defend.

---

## The Process

**Step 1** — each participant reads and internalises their role. They identify what their character needs and what they are prepared to concede.

**Step 2** — opening statements. Each participant presents their position without interruption.

**Step 3** — negotiation. The group works toward a proposal that addresses the core interests of all roles. No role is silenced; no interest is ignored.

**Step 4** — shared proposal. The group produces one written position that all members sign. Not a compromise of indifference — a genuine collective position.

**Step 5** — debrief. The group reflects on the process: where was agreement easy? Where was it hard? What did you have to let go of? What did you learn from a position that was not your own?

---

## Why It Matters

The Consensus Challenge is a low-risk rehearsal for high-stakes professional and civic situations: budget meetings, curriculum committees, school councils, community consultations. The skills it develops — active listening, position-taking, concession, synthesis — are transferable.
