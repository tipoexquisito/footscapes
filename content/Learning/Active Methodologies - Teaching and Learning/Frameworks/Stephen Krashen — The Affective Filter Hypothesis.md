Stephen Krashen (1941–), American linguist, proposed the Affective Filter Hypothesis as part of his broader Input Hypothesis model of second language acquisition.

---

## The Core Argument

Language acquisition is blocked by emotional states. Three affective variables determine how much comprehensible input a learner is able to receive and process:

**Motivation** — learners who want to learn acquire language more successfully. Extrinsic pressure alone does not produce acquisition.

**Self-confidence** — learners who believe they can succeed maintain a lower filter and engage more freely with input.

**Anxiety** — high anxiety raises the affective filter, blocking input from reaching the language acquisition device even when it is comprehensible.

When the filter is low, input gets through. When the filter is high, learners may be present and receiving language but not acquiring it.

---

## Implications for Classroom Practice

The classroom environment is not neutral. An anxious, competitive, or humiliating environment raises the affective filter for every learner — particularly for those who already carry additional barriers: students with SEND, learners with little L1 literacy, recent immigrants, or anyone whose identity is not reflected in the curriculum.

Creating a low-filter environment — through trust, inclusion, routine, and genuine respect for prior knowledge — is not pastoral care. It is the prerequisite for learning.
