Howard Gardner (1943–), American developmental psychologist, proposed in *Frames of Mind* (1983) that intelligence is not a single, measurable capacity but a set of distinct cognitive abilities.

---

## The Eight Intelligences

Gardner identified eight distinct intelligences, each representing a different way of processing information and solving problems:

**Verbal-Linguistic** — sensitivity to language, meaning, and structure. Reading, writing, storytelling, debate.

**Logical-Mathematical** — capacity for reasoning, pattern recognition, and abstract thinking. Problem-solving, classification, numerical logic.

**Visual-Spatial** — ability to think in images, navigate space, and perceive and recreate visual information. Drawing, design, architecture, navigation.

**Bodily-Kinesthetic** — use of the body to solve problems and express ideas. Sport, dance, craft, surgery, performance.

**Musical-Rhythmic** — sensitivity to pitch, rhythm, tone, and musical structure. Singing, playing instruments, composition, musical memory.

**Interpersonal** — capacity to understand other people's intentions, motivations, and emotions. Teaching, leadership, counselling, collaboration.

**Intrapersonal** — access to one's own emotional life, capacity for self-reflection. Metacognition, emotional regulation, personal goal-setting.

**Naturalistic** — ability to recognise, categorise, and connect patterns in the natural world. Biology, ecology, agriculture, environmental awareness.

---

## The Pedagogical Argument

No single intelligence is more valuable than others. Schooling has traditionally privileged the first two — but most professions, and most human lives, depend on a broader profile.

Gardner's framework supports **differentiated instruction** and [[Universal Design for Learning]]: designing learning that offers multiple ways in, not a single path that advantages one type of thinker.
