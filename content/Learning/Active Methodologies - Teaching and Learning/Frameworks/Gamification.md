Gamification applies game mechanics to non-game contexts to increase motivation, engagement, and participation. It is not about making learning a game — it is about using the logic of games (challenge, feedback, progression, competition, reward) to shape the learning environment.

Used well, gamification reduces affective barriers, increases voluntary participation, and creates social cohesion within a group.

---

## In Practice

The Images of Madrid scavenger hunt on Day 1 used several game mechanics simultaneously:

- **Teams** — group identity and shared investment
- **Challenge** — a defined task with a time limit
- **Discovery** — unknown outcomes; the city as the board
- **Competition** — implicit, without stakes that exclude
- **Shared debriefing** — the game as entry point for reflection

The bonus challenge structure used during the week worked similarly: optional participation, visible recognition, self-directed engagement.

---

> [!concept] Gamification and intrinsic motivation
> The risk of gamification is substituting external reward for intrinsic interest. Points and prizes can crowd out curiosity. The approach works best when the game structure serves a learning goal that participants already find meaningful — not when it replaces meaning with mechanics.
