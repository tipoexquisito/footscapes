Urban pedagogy treats the city as a learning environment — not a backdrop for learning, but an active site of knowledge. The built environment, public space, human flow, commercial culture, and historical sediment all carry meaning that can be read, interpreted, and learned from.

The educator's role in this context is the [[Notes on Curating Learning|curator]]: selecting which spaces to enter, framing the question to bring to them, and leaving room for the learner's own encounter.

---

## The City as Text

A city can be read. Its architecture, naming conventions, commercial signage, street art, and patterns of use all encode social, political, and historical information. Urban pedagogy develops the literacy to read these signs — not abstractly, but while walking through them.

Key capacities it develops:
- spatial awareness and orientation
- observation and attention to detail
- semiotic reading (signs, symbols, representations)
- historical inference from material culture
- critical questioning of what is visible and what is not

## In Practice

The Images of Madrid walking tour (Day 1) was structured urban pedagogy. Participants moved through the city with a task, a group, and a defined observational frame. The city was the classroom. The scavenger hunt format used game mechanics to keep the inquiry active. See [[Gamification]], [[Place-based Learning]].
