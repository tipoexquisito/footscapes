Temporary, calibrated support that enables a learner to perform something they could not yet do alone — and that is progressively withdrawn as competence develops.

---

## The Origin

The concept was developed by Jerome Bruner, David Wood, and Gail Ross in the 1970s, building directly on [[Vygotsky — Zone of Proximal Development]]'s insight that learning happens at the edge of current capacity, not within it.

The metaphor is structural: a builder's scaffold holds a wall in place while the mortar sets, then comes down. The scaffold is not the building. It is what makes the building possible. See [[Scaffolding (Seamus Heaney)]].

---

## What Scaffolding Is Not

Scaffolding is not simplification. Reducing difficulty is not scaffolding — it is just making things easier. Scaffolding keeps the challenge and provides support for reaching it.

Scaffolding is not permanent. Support that never withdraws produces dependence, not competence. The goal of every scaffold is its own removal.

---

## Forms of Scaffolding in Practice

Verbal scaffolding — questioning, modelling, narrating thinking aloud.
Visual scaffolding — diagrams, graphic organisers, sentence frames.
Peer scaffolding — a more capable peer working alongside a learner at their ZPD.
Environmental scaffolding — designing the learning space and sequence to make progression possible.
Technological scaffolding — digital tools that mediate access to complex content.
