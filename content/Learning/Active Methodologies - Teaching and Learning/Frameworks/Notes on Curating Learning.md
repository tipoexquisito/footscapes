[[Curation]] is selection with intention.

A curator does not create the objects in their care. They select, sequence, contextualise, and leave space for the visitor's own encounter with what is there.

The strongest teaching observed during the course operated on the same logic. The educator was not the source of meaning — they were the designer of conditions in which meaning could emerge.

---

## What Curating Learning Looks Like

A learning curator makes decisions about:

- **Selection** — which experiences, objects, spaces, and questions to bring into the learning environment
- **Sequence** — in what order, at what pace, with what gaps between
- **Framing** — how much to explain before the encounter, and how much to leave for the encounter itself
- **Space** — where learning happens shapes what can be learned
- **Silence** — what not to say, and when not to speak

The course introduced this concretely through the [[Audio Guide as Scaffolding]] exercise at the [[Highlights of Spanish Art|Prado]]: participants wrote their own audio guides for a painting. To explain something to someone else, you first have to decide what matters. The exercise collapsed the distance between interpreter and object.

The Prado visit worked as curated learning. A single room, a single painting, time to look before speaking. The educator's job was to stay out of the way until the learner had seen something.

---

## The Risk of Over-Curating

A curator who labels everything, explains every object, and leaves no room for the visitor's own reading has stopped curating and started narrating. The same applies in teaching.

Over-scaffolding collapses the [[Vygotsky — Zone of Proximal Development|ZPD]]. Over-explanation eliminates inquiry. The educator who cannot tolerate ambiguity in learners tends to fill silence that should belong to the learner.

> Curating learning requires sustained self-observation. The educator must notice when they are explaining out of habit rather than necessity, when they are filling silence out of discomfort, and when they are choosing activities for their own satisfaction rather than the learner's growth. ## The Hidden Curriculum of the Course

The course design itself was a model of curated learning. The educators did not teach about active methodologies — they enacted them. Every session, field visit, and transition was designed. Nothing was arbitrary.

This is perhaps the most transferable lesson from the week: the curriculum is not the slides. It is the entire designed experience — including the walk to the museum, the choice of restaurant, the moment of open time in the city.
