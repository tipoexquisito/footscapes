Three approaches to group formation, each with different pedagogical implications. Based on Johnson, Johnson and Holubec (1994).

---

## Random Groups

Participants are grouped by chance: drawing numbers, matching colours, counting off. No one chooses; no one is chosen.

Random grouping works against cliques, challenges comfort zones, and produces diverse configurations. It is the appropriate choice when the goal is exposure to different perspectives or when the teacher wants to prevent fixed social hierarchies from shaping the work.

---

## Teacher-Selected Groups

The teacher assigns groups intentionally, based on knowledge of learners: balancing skills, mixing languages, separating particular combinations, or deliberately pairing a more able learner with one who would benefit from peer support.

Teacher selection is appropriate when differentiation is a goal, when the task requires specific skill complementarity, or when social dynamics need to be managed.

---

## Self-Selected Groups

Participants choose their own groups. This maximises comfort and existing trust, which can produce more ambitious or risk-tolerant work among established pairs. It also tends to reproduce existing social divisions.

Self-selection is appropriate when the task requires deep collaboration and when the learning goal is not disruption of existing patterns.

---

## The Principle

No method is universally correct. The choice of group formation method is a pedagogical decision that should be made in relation to the specific task, the specific group, and the specific moment of the learning sequence.

Varying the method across a programme is itself a teaching move.
