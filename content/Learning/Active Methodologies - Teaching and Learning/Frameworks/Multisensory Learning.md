Multisensory Learning engages more than one sensory channel simultaneously — visual, auditory, kinaesthetic, olfactory, tactile — to produce deeper encoding and more durable retention.

The premise: when multiple senses are activated together, the brain forms richer associative networks. Memory becomes more accessible because it is anchored in more than one modality.

---

## In Practice

The course activated multisensory learning across almost every field experience.

**At the Prado**: visual attention + verbal description + spatial navigation. Standing in front of Las Meninas at full scale, in real light, is not the same as seeing a reproduction on screen.

**In Toledo**: sound of stone streets, smell of old buildings, spatial compression of medieval architecture, the scale of El entierro del conde de Orgaz in the small church where it was painted.

**Mindfulness exercises**: breath, body, sound, texture. The five senses used deliberately as anchors for attention.

**Gastronomy**: taste, smell, and memory as a single event. Food as a multisensory cultural text.

---

> [!concept] Multisensory is not decoration
> Engaging multiple senses is not about making lessons more entertaining. It is about providing more retrieval routes for the same information. A learner who tasted, heard, and saw something has more pathways back to it than a learner who only read about it.
