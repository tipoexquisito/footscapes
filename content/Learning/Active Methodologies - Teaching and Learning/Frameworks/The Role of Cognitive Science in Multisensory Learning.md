The cognitive science basis for multisensory learning rests primarily on **dual coding theory**, developed by Allan Paivio (1971).

---

## Dual Coding Theory

Paivio proposed that the brain uses two distinct but interconnected systems to process information: a **verbal system** (for language and linguistic information) and a **non-verbal system** (for images, spatial information, and sensory input).

When information is encoded through both systems simultaneously — when a concept is read and also seen as an image, heard and also felt physically — the resulting memory trace is stronger, more distributed, and more easily retrieved.

The implication for learning design: combining verbal and visual (or other sensory) modes does not simply add variety. It produces qualitatively richer encoding.

---

## The Working Memory Connection

Working memory has limited capacity. Overloading a single channel (presenting dense text while also speaking, for example) degrades processing. Distributing information across multiple sensory channels makes more efficient use of available cognitive capacity.

Multisensory design is, among other things, cognitive load management.
