The case for multisensory learning is grounded in cognitive science, not pedagogical fashion.

---

## Memory and Recall

The brain encodes information through multiple pathways simultaneously. When an experience engages sight, sound, touch, smell, and movement together, it creates a richer and more interconnected memory trace. Retrieval is easier because there are more routes back to the same information.

A single-channel input (reading alone, or listening alone) produces thinner encoding. Multisensory input produces denser, more durable memory.

---

## Engagement and Attention

Sensory variety sustains attention. A learning environment that shifts between visual, auditory, kinaesthetic, and social modes reduces the cognitive fatigue that comes from sustained single-channel processing.

Novelty and sensory change are neurologically alerting — they signal that something worth attending to is happening.

---

## Inclusion

Multisensory design is inherently more inclusive. Learners with dyslexia, ADHD, processing differences, or limited language access benefit significantly from alternative entry points. What begins as an accommodation becomes a design principle that improves learning for everyone.

This connects directly to [[Universal Design for Learning]]: design for the margins, benefit the centre.
