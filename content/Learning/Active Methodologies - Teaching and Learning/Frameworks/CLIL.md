Framework by Mehisto and Lucietto (2010). Source: [CLIL Principles](https://www.icfabriani.edu.it/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/WHAT-IS-CLIL-BY-PROF.SSA-SANDRA-LUCIETTO.pdf)

CLIL is a dual-focus approach in which learners acquire subject content and an additional language simultaneously. The emphasis is not on the language alone but on knowledge construction, critical thinking, and learning competences.

CLIL is not simply teaching a subject in English. It should not be elitist, selective, or a replacement for the mother tongue. Language learning happens through authentic, cognitively relevant tasks.

> [!concept] The 4 Cs of CLIL
> **Content** — subject knowledge and skills. **Communication** — language as a tool for learning, not just a subject. **Cognition** — higher-order thinking embedded in tasks. **Culture** — intercultural awareness and the European dimension. These four are inseparable in a CLIL lesson.

---

## The 12 Principles

**1. Managing the Affective Side.** Learning complex content in an additional language generates anxiety. The teacher must build a safe, motivating, inclusive environment — no labelling, no ridicule, high expectations for all. Emotional security is a prerequisite for linguistic and cognitive risk-taking.

**2. Making Intended Learning Explicit and Visible.** Learners need to know what they are learning, why it matters, and how they will demonstrate it. Objectives must integrate content, language, and learning skills. Visibility fosters autonomy and self-regulation.

**3. Making Academic Language Visible.** Academic language cannot be assumed. Teachers must explicitly teach disciplinary vocabulary, discourse structures, connectors, and registers. Language is a cognitive tool, not an isolated list of words.

**4. Reflection on Learning.** Learners reflect not just on content but on the language they used and the strategies that worked. This builds Metacognition, linguistic awareness, and continuous improvement. Learning becomes a conscious process, not just task completion.

**5. Fostering Critical Thinking.** CLIL should not stop at memorisation. Learners analyse, evaluate, create, and solve problems. Mehisto and Lucietto connect this explicitly to Bloom's revised taxonomy: deeper thinking requires more complex language. Cognition and language are interdependent.

**6. Taking Time for Meaningful Learning.** Depth over coverage. Learning becomes meaningful when it connects to prior experience, crosses disciplines, and allows application of ideas. Surface learning produces temporary retention; meaningful learning produces transfer.

**7. Scaffolding Content.** Temporary cognitive support — graphic organisers, analogies, modelling, chunking of complex tasks — allows learners to access content even when language is still developing. Support is gradual and progressively removed as autonomy grows. See [[Scaffolding]].

**8. Scaffolding Language.** Alongside content, language itself needs structured support: sentence frames, writing frames, visual tables, pre-teaching of vocabulary. Linguistic scaffolding must be functional and contextualised, never decontextualised vocabulary lists.

**9. Cooperative Learning.** CLIL values structured collaborative work. Positive interdependence, individual accountability, and promotive interaction generate more authentic oral use of the language, reduce anxiety, and build knowledge together. See [[Cooperative Learning]].

**10. Fostering Learner Autonomy.** The CLIL learner must become progressively more independent — making decisions, choosing strategies, monitoring progress. The teacher negotiates, offers choices, and teaches study strategies. Autonomy is a lifelong learning competence.

**11. Connecting with CLIL Language Speakers and Cultures.** CLIL promotes authentic intercultural contact through eTwinning, exchanges, online partnerships, and cross-cultural analysis. Language stops being a school subject and becomes a medium for real communication across cultures.

**12. Asymmetry in Classroom Talk in Favour of Students.** Learners should speak more than the teacher. Less presentational talk, more exploratory talk — discussing, negotiating, hypothesising, arguing. Language is learned by using it actively in cognitively relevant tasks.

---

## What CLIL Is, at Its Core

Mehisto and Lucietto present CLIL as pedagogical, inclusive, cognitively demanding, linguistically supported, collaborative, reflective, and intercultural. The goal is not to teach content in another language. It is to create deep, meaningful, multimodal learning experiences.

> [!quote] The core distinction
> "CLIL is not about teaching a subject in English. It is about using language to think, to learn, and to connect with others across cultures."
