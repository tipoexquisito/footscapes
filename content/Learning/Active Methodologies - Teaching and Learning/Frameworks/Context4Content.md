*Context precedes content. Experience precedes explanation. Observation precedes abstraction.*

Context4Content is the methodological axis that ran through the entire course. Its premise: learning does not need to be applied to reality — it is constructed from reality. Space, culture, and lived experience are not illustrations of content. They are the content.

This shifts the educator's role from content deliverer to [[Notes on Curating Learning|learning curator]], and the learner from receiver to active interpreter of context.

---

## Conceptual Foundations

Context4Content draws on several established frameworks:

[[Situated Learning]] — knowledge is context-dependent and socially constructed. Knowing and doing are inseparable.

[[Experiential Learning]] — learning through direct experience and reflection. The Kolb cycle (experience → reflection → conceptualisation → application) maps onto the field learning structure of the course.

[[Place-based Learning]] — the environment as pedagogical agent. The city, the museum, the street are not backdrops. They are teachers.

[[Constructivist Pedagogy]] — meaning is actively built, not transmitted. Each learner constructs understanding through interaction with context.

[[Learning/Active Methodologies - Teaching and Learning/Frameworks/Reflective Practice]] — learning becomes visible through reflection. Without reflection, experience remains raw material.

---

## The Learning Cycle

Context4Content operates through a repeating cycle:

1. **Immersion** — entering the context without agenda
2. **Observation** — attending to what is there (visual, sensory, spatial, cultural)
3. **Interpretation** — asking what it might mean
4. **Articulation** — expressing learning in a chosen form (written, visual, spoken)
5. **Reflection** — stepping back to name what was learned and how

Each pass through the cycle deepens both the understanding of place and the understanding of pedagogy.

## Applications in the Course

**Madrid** — the city as living curriculum. Urban observation as [[Visual Learning]]; El Rastro as ethnographic field site; Casa de Campo as ecological perception; Sala Equis as cultural repurposing of space.

**[[Highlights of Spanish Art]]** — art history as interpretative practice. Symbolism, power, and historical framing read through composition and visual culture.

**[[Museo Nacional Centro de Arte Reina Sofia]]** — contemporary art and critical thinking. Disruption of narrative conventions; political and social interpretation.

**[[Toledo — The City of Three Cultures]]** — intercultural historical synthesis. Urban palimpsest of civilisations; memory, identity, and historical layering.

**IES Príncipe Felipe** — the school as field site. Classroom observation as ethnography; institutional culture as a learning object.

**Gastronomy** — food as identity system. Culinary traditions as historical layering; sensory learning through taste and memory.

## Field Notes as Methodology

Field notes in this framework are not descriptive logs. They are thinking instruments. A field note includes:

- sensory impressions — what is seen, heard, felt
- analytical annotations — what it might mean
- questions generated by observation
- connections to pedagogy or curriculum

Useful prompts for field note practice:
- What does this space teach without speaking?
- What assumptions does this environment challenge?
- How would students interpret this differently?
- What curriculum is embedded here, invisibly?
