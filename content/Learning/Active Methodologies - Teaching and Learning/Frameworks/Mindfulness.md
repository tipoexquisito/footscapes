Mindfulness in an educational context is the practice of deliberate, non-judgmental attention — to breath, body, sensation, and present experience. It is used to regulate affect, reduce cognitive overload, and bring learners fully into the present moment before engaging with learning tasks.

It is not relaxation. It is attention training.

---

## Why It Matters in Learning

The affective state of the learner conditions what is learned. A group arriving anxious, distracted, or dysregulated cannot engage at the same level as one that is settled and present. Brief mindfulness practices at the start of a session shift the group's baseline.

This connects directly to [[Stephen Krashen — The Affective Filter Hypothesis]]: when the affective filter is high (anxiety, self-consciousness, fear), input does not reach the learner. Mindfulness lowers the filter.

## Practices Used in the Course

The course integrated several brief mindfulness and grounding practices:

- **[[5-4-3-2-1 Grounding Exercise]]** — sensory anchoring through five senses
- **[[A Mindfulness Moment — Using Our Senses to Feel Grounded]]** — sensory awareness practice
- **[[Beach Ball Breathing Technique]]** — breath regulation through visual and kinaesthetic cue
- **[[Box Breathing Technique]]** — structured breath pattern for calm focus
- **[[Marzipan Time — A Mindfulness Exercise]]** — sensory memory and imaginative engagement

---

> [!concept] Mindfulness as transition
> Mindfulness moments work best as transitions — between arrival and work, between activity and reflection, between a challenging moment and the next task. They mark a shift in register, and that marking is itself pedagogically significant.
