Reflective practice, developed by Donald Schön (1983), describes the capacity of professionals to examine their own actions in order to engage in a process of continuous learning. It is not self-criticism — it is disciplined self-observation in service of growth.

Schön distinguished two forms:

- **Reflection-in-action**: thinking while doing; adjusting in the moment
- **Reflection-on-action**: thinking after the fact; making sense of what happened

> "The practitioner allows himself to experience surprise, puzzlement, or confusion in a situation which he finds uncertain or unique. He reflects on the phenomenon before him, and on the prior understandings which have been implicit in his behaviour."
> — Schön, *The Reflective Practitioner* (1983, p. 68)

---

## In the Course

Reflection was woven into the structure of the week rather than added at the end. Field notes, learning portfolios, cooperative debriefs, and the format of this digital garden are all forms of reflective practice.

The assumption was that experience without reflection does not become learning. It becomes memory without meaning.

---

> [!concept] Reflection is not summary
> Summarising what happened is not reflection. Reflection asks what it meant, what was unexpected, what it reveals about assumptions, and what it might change in future practice. The question is not "what did I do?" but "what did I learn about learning?"
