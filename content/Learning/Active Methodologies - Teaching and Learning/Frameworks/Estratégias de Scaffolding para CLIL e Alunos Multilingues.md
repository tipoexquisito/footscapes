Eight evidence-based strategies for supporting learners who are accessing content through a language they are still acquiring. Applicable in CLIL classrooms and any multilingual learning environment.

---

## The Eight Strategies

**1. Visuals and Realia** — Images, objects, diagrams, and real-world materials reduce dependence on verbal explanation. When the concept can be seen, touched, or handled, comprehension does not depend entirely on linguistic decoding.

**2. Modelling** — Demonstrating the task or process before asking learners to attempt it. The teacher or a capable peer performs the thinking visibly, narrating the steps. This provides a cognitive map before independent performance.

**3. Activating Prior Knowledge** — Beginning from what learners already know — in any language — before introducing new content. Comprehension is built on connection. See [[David Ausubel — Meaningful Learning]].

**4. Graphic Organisers** — Visual structures (mind maps, Venn diagrams, T-charts, timelines) that organise and make relationships between ideas visible. They reduce the cognitive load of prose and provide an alternative encoding route.

**5. Sentence Frames** — Partial structures that support spoken and written language production: *"I agree with... because..."*, *"One difference is..."*, *"The evidence suggests..."*. They scaffold academic register without removing the learner's voice.

**6. Read-Aloud** — Reading aloud to learners — with explicit attention to vocabulary, rhythm, and meaning — supports comprehension and models fluent academic language. Particularly valuable in CLIL and language-intensive subjects.

**7. Small Groups and Peer Support** — Collaborative structures reduce anxiety, allow learners to negotiate meaning in lower-stakes settings, and create natural opportunities for peer modelling. See [[Cooperative Learning]] and [[Stephen Krashen — The Affective Filter Hypothesis]].

**8. Strategic Use of the Mother Tongue** — Allowing learners to think, plan, or clarify in their first language before producing in the target language. The L1 is a cognitive resource, not a problem to be eliminated. Translanguaging is a legitimate and effective strategy in multilingual classrooms.
