A cooperative critical thinking technique in which groups rank nine quotations or statements by arranging them in a diamond pattern — one at the top (most important), two below, three in the middle, two below that, and one at the bottom (least important, or most contested).

---

## The Structure

```
        [1]
      [2] [3]
    [4] [5] [6]
      [7] [8]
        [9]
```

The arrangement is not a simple ranking — it is a spatial argument. The three in the middle are "neither central nor marginal." The two at the bottom are not necessarily wrong — they are the statements the group found hardest to place at the top.

## The Nine Quotations Used in the Course

The quotations used during the programme centred on education, learning, and the future:

"Education is the most powerful weapon you can use to change the world." — source debated.

"Tell me and I forget. Teach me and I remember. Involve me and I learn." — attributed to Benjamin Franklin, origin debated.

"The function of education is to teach one to think intensively and to think critically." — Martin Luther King Jr.

"Education is not filling a bucket but lighting a fire." — W.B. Yeats (attributed).

"Intelligence plus character — that is the goal of true education." — Martin Luther King Jr.

"The mediator between head and hands must be the heart." — Fritz Lang, *Metropolis*.

"Every child is an artist. The problem is how to remain an artist once we grow up." — [[Picasso]].

"If we teach today as we taught yesterday, we rob our children of tomorrow." — John Dewey.

"The quill is the tongue of the soul." — [[Cervantes]].

---

## What the Technique Develops

Argumentation — the group must defend each placement. Active listening — disagreement must be resolved through dialogue, not volume. Tolerance for ambiguity — the diamond structure allows for "important but complex" rather than forcing binary judgement. Critical thinking — the process reveals which values each participant holds most centrally.
