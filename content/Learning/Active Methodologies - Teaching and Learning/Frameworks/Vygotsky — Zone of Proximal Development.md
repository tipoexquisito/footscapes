Lev Vygotsky (1896–1934), Soviet psychologist. His concept of the Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD) remains one of the most generative frameworks in educational theory.

> “Proximal refers to skills the learner is close to mastering.”

---

## The Zone of Proximal Development

> "What a child can do with assistance today, she will be able to do by herself tomorrow."

The ZPD is the space between what a learner can do independently and what they can do with the support of a more capable other — a teacher, peer, or well-designed tool.

> “The zone of proximal development refers to the difference between what a learner can do without help and what he or she can achieve with guidance and encouragement from a skilled partner.”

Learning does not happen at the level of what is already mastered. It happens at the edge: where the task is just beyond current reach, but within reach with support.

> "Learning is more than the acquisition of the ability to think; it is the acquisition of many specialized abilities for thinking about a variety of things."

---

> [!concept] The ZPD in one image
> Think of three concentric circles. The innermost: what the learner can do alone. The middle ring: the Zone of Proximal Development — what they can do with support. The outer ring: what is not yet reachable. Learning happens in the middle ring. The teacher's job is to work there.

## Scaffolding as the Operative Concept

The ZPD implies **scaffolding**: temporary, calibrated support that enables the learner to perform what they could not perform alone — and that is withdrawn progressively as competence develops.

Good scaffolding is not simplification. It is structured support at the right level of challenge.

> [!connection] ZPD and the peer
> Vygotsky's "more capable other" does not have to be the teacher. A peer who is slightly further ahead sits inside the learner's ZPD and can provide exactly the right level of challenge. This is the theoretical foundation of [[Cooperative Learning]].
