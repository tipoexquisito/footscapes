Numbered Heads Together is a Cooperative Learning structure developed by Spencer Kagan. It combines individual accountability with group interdependence: every member must be prepared to respond, because any one of them may be called upon.

**How it works**

1. Students are organised into small groups. Each member receives a number (1, 2, 3, 4).
2. The teacher poses a question or problem.
3. Groups discuss and ensure every member understands and can explain the answer.
4. The teacher calls a number at random. The student with that number in each group responds.

The random selection is the structural key: it prevents the group from delegating response to its strongest member. Everyone must be ready.

**Why it matters pedagogically**

Numbered Heads Together operationalises two of the five pillars of [[Cooperative Learning]]: positive interdependence (the group succeeds or fails together) and individual accountability (no one can coast on another's work).

Questions the technique tends to surface in practice:
- How do you form groups that balance ability and participation?
- When has cooperative learning worked well in your classroom, and what made it work?
- How do you maintain individual accountability without undermining group trust?
- What are the main challenges, and how do you address them?
