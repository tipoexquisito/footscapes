Students or teacher prepare questions about a topic and the person 'being interviewed' must answer them.

Take turns in being the interviewee and interviewer.

---
### Topic: What forms of assessment do you use?

1. **Pair Interviews**
	Students pair up.
	Student A interviews Student B on a specific topic for a set time.

2. **Role Reversal**
	Pairs reverse roles;
	Student B interviews Student A for the same amount of time.

3. **Team Sharing**
	Pairs join another pair to form a group of four.
	Each student shares their partner's findings with the group.

---

The "Team Interview" is a highly interactive activity designed to build communication skills, active listening, and empathy. Students or teachers write a list of questions about an agreed-upon topic (e.g., historical events, science concepts, or personal hobbies) and take turns acting as the interviewer and interviewee.

---

1. **How to Set Up the Activity**

- **Preparation:** Have the teacher or students brainstorm a list of 5 to 10 open-ended questions based on the chosen theme.
- **Pair Up:** Divide the group into pairs. Assign one person to be the "Interviewer" and the other to be the "Interviewee."
- **First Round:** The interviewer asks the prepared questions. The interviewee must answer thoughtfully, and the interviewer can take notes or ask spontaneous follow-up questions.
- **Switch Roles:** After a set time (e.g., 5 to 10 minutes), the pair switches roles so the original interviewee gets a turn in the hot seat.

---

2. **Example Topic Ideas & Questions**

Depending on the learning objective, you can tailor the questions to test knowledge or build relationships:

- **Academic/Subject-Focused:**
    - "If you were a historical figure from the [specific era], what would be your biggest priority?"
    - "How does the water cycle work in real life, and why is it important?"
- **Communication & Personal Interests:**
    - "What is one goal you are hoping to accomplish this year, and why?"
    - "If you could only eat one food for the rest of your life, what would it be?" [[1](https://miro.com/blog/ice-breaker-questions-for-team/)]

3. Alternative Formats

To keep the activity dynamic, try these variations:

- **The Three-Step Interview:** Students work in groups of three. Student A interviews Student B, Student B answers, and Student C takes notes. They rotate until everyone has played all three roles.
- **Speed Interviews:** Participants form two lines facing each other. They have 3 minutes to interview the person across from them. When the timer sounds, one line shifts down to interview a new partner.
