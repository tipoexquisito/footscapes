Constructivism holds that learners build knowledge actively — through experience, reflection, and interaction with their environment — rather than receiving it passively from an external source. Knowledge is constructed, not transmitted.

The tradition draws on Piaget (individual cognitive development through assimilation and accommodation), Vygotsky (social construction of meaning through interaction), and Dewey (learning through experience and reflection).

---

## Key Implications for Teaching

- Prior knowledge matters: new learning connects to what the learner already knows. See [[David Ausubel — Meaningful Learning]].
- The learner is an agent, not a recipient.
- Error is part of the process, not a sign of failure.
- Social interaction produces understanding that individual study does not.
- Context shapes what can be learned. See [[Situated Learning]].

---

## In the Course

Constructivist principles were embedded in the course design without being named as such. The learners were consistently asked to interpret, not to receive. The educators structured conditions for meaning-making — they did not deliver meaning pre-packaged.
