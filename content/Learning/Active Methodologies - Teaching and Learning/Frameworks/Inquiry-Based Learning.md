Inquiry-Based Learning (IBL) positions the learner as investigator. Rather than receiving answers, learners generate questions — and the process of seeking answers is the learning.

The approach shifts the authority in the classroom: the teacher becomes a guide who structures conditions for discovery, not a source who transmits settled knowledge.

---

## Core Principle

Learning begins with a question, not a statement. The learner observes, notices something that doesn't fit, asks why, and investigates. Understanding is built through the inquiry process itself, not deposited in advance.

---

## In Practice

Inquiry-Based Learning appeared consistently during the course — often without being named as such.

**At the Prado**: interpreting artworks without prior explanation. Reading composition, inferring narrative, questioning authorship and context.

**In Toledo**: historical inference from urban space. What does this building tell us about who lived here? What is still visible, and what has been erased?

**In cooperative tasks**: guided discovery within group structures. Each participant brought a piece; the inquiry was collective.

---

> [!concept] Inquiry is not the absence of structure
> IBL does not mean unguided exploration. The educator designs the conditions — the right object, the right question, the right moment of silence — so that the learner's inquiry has somewhere to go.
