The audio guide — in a museum, a city, or a cultural space — is one of the oldest forms of pedagogical scaffolding in informal learning environments.

---

## What It Does

An audio guide provides narration, context, and structured interpretation for an experience that would otherwise require prior knowledge the visitor may not have. It does not replace the experience; it mediates it.

In UDL terms, the audio guide is a **means of representation**: it offers a verbal pathway to meaning alongside the visual and spatial experience of the work itself. See [[Universal Design for Learning]].

In CLIL terms, it is **language support within content learning**: the learner accesses the subject (art history, cultural heritage, urban history) through a language they may be still acquiring. See [[CLIL]] and [[Estratégias de Scaffolding para CLIL e Alunos Multilingues]].

---

## The Pedagogical Argument

A well-designed audio guide does what good scaffolding always does: it enables access to a level of understanding the learner could not reach alone — and it does so without simplifying the content, only the route to it.

The goal is not to make the work easier. It is to make it navigable.

---

## The Teacher as Audio Guide

The metaphor extends to classroom practice. The teacher who contextualises, narrates, and connects — without interpreting for the learner or removing the difficulty of the encounter — is functioning as a pedagogical audio guide.

Scaffolding is not telling people what to think. It is helping them build the conditions for thinking.
