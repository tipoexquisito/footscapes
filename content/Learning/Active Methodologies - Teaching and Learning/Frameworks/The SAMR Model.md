Developed by Ruben Puentedura, SAMR describes four levels at which technology integrates into learning — from simple replacement to genuine transformation.

---

## The Four Levels

**Substitution** — technology acts as a direct substitute for an existing tool, with no functional change. A student types an essay instead of handwriting it.

**Augmentation** — technology acts as a substitute with some functional improvement. The same essay now has spell-check, word count, and easy revision.

**Modification** — technology allows for significant task redesign. The essay becomes a collaborative document, edited simultaneously by peers, with comment threads and revision history.

**Redefinition** — technology creates tasks that were previously inconceivable. The student publishes to a real audience, receives responses from across Europe, and iterates based on genuine feedback.

---

## The Distinction That Matters

The first two levels (Substitution and Augmentation) involve **enhancement** — doing the same things better. The second two (Modification and Redefinition) involve **transformation** — doing things that were not previously possible.

SAMR is not a hierarchy of virtue: Substitution is sometimes the right tool. The question is whether the choice is intentional and appropriate.
