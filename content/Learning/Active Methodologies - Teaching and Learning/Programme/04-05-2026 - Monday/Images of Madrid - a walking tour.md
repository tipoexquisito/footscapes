### 1. Plaza Mayor

The Plaza Mayor was once the centre of old Madrid. It was completed in 1620 and in the past was used for many purposes, including bullfights, processions, fiestas and theatrical productions, trials of the Inquisition, and even executions.

### 3. Plaza de la Villa

This square has some of the oldest buildings in the city and was one of the main medieval centres of Madrid.

The oldest building is thought to be the tower of the Lujanes. The legend of this tower is that King Francis I of France was kept captive here in 1525 after losing in battle to Carlos I. When Francis I refused to bow to Carlos I, the king of Spain had the entrance doorway lowered so that Francis was forced to bow every time he left or entered the building.

### 4. Almudena cathedral

Building work on the Almudena cathedral started in 1893, but it was not completed until 1993. One of the reasons for this delay was the civil war in the 1930s.

If you walk north past the main cathedral entrance, you will see a square on your left. On your right you have the back of the palace, and to your left you have the cathedral de Almudena.

### 5. The Royal Palace

The Royal Palace of Madrid is the largest in Western Europe and one of the largest in the world. It was built by Carlos V, the first king in the Bourbon dynasty (the same as the current Spanish Royal family. With over 135,000 square metres and 3,418 rooms, it has witnessed centuries of Spanish history.

Outside the palace there is a plaque commemorating heroes from ‘2 de mayo de 1808’

### 6. Plaza de Oriente

From Plaza de Oriente you can see the palace and the Royal Opera House. The man on the statue is Philip IV. When he ordered the statue to be constructed, he wanted the horse to be rearing, which was very unusual for the time. The Italian sculptor Tacca couldn’t think of a way to achieve this (so that the statue wouldn’t fall over), so he wrote to Galileo Galilei for help.

### 7. San Gines chocolateria

A traditional snack in Madrid is a cup of hot chocolate with churros (a type of long doughnut). 

Cacao was brought back to Spain from South America in the 16th century. People then adapted it by adding sugar and cinnamon. By the 17th century thick, hot chocolate was very popular with the Spanish elite and then later with the general public. 

The San Gines chocolateria is one of the most famous places to try this. If you have time to spare you could try some!

### 8. Sol 

The Puerta del Sol (gate of the sun) is one of the most famous squares in Madrid and is a common meeting place for both tourists and residents. In the centre is a statue of Carlos III. The large white building was built in the 18th century and was built as Madrid’s central post office. 

Since the 1990s it has been the headquarters of Madrid’s regional government. 

The bear and strawberry tree statue represents the Madrid coat of arms. Medieval Madrid had lots of forests and bears were very common. The ‘strawberry’ tree is in fact an unedo tree. This is a tree which is common to Spain and Portugal and has small red fruits which can be used to make liqueur.
