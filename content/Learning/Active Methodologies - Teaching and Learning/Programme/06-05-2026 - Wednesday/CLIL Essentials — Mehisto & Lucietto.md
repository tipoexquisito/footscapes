# CLIL Essentials — Mehisto & Lucietto (2010/2011)

Reference framework for quality CLIL design. Distributed as supplementary reading in connection with the CLIL sessions.

The framework presents CLIL not as a method to be applied but as a set of conditions to be maintained. Twelve principles, each pointing at a different dimension of what makes CLIL work — and what makes it fail when absent.

---

## What CLIL Is Not

Before the principles: the framework is explicit about what CLIL is not.

It is not simply delivering a subject in English. It is not an elitist approach for high achievers. It does not replace or devalue the first language. Linguistic acquisition happens through cognitively authentic tasks, not through exposure alone.

---

## The Twelve Principles

**1. Managing the Affective Side.** Learning content in an additional language can generate anxiety, self-consciousness and fear of error. The classroom must be a safe space: high expectations for all, no ridicule, no sarcasm, the error treated as part of the process rather than evidence of failure. The affective filter (Krashen) applies here directly.

**2. Making Intended Learning Explicit and Visible.** Students need to know what they are learning, why it matters, and how they will demonstrate it. In CLIL, objectives must be dual: content and language. "We are studying volcanoes" is not a learning objective. "We will identify tectonic plates and explain their interactions using scientific analogy" is.

**3. Making Academic Language Visible.** Conversational fluency and academic language proficiency are not the same thing (BICS/CALP). The CLIL teacher explicitly teaches: discipline-specific vocabulary, connectors, argumentative structures, formal registers. Academic language is treated as a cognitive tool, not an ornament.

**4. Reflecting on Learning.** Metacognition is built into the lesson structure, not added at the end. Reflection covers content, language and learning strategies: What did I learn? What language did I use? What worked? What was difficult?

**5. Fostering Critical Thinking.** CLIL must not reduce to memorisation. Bloom's taxonomy revised: Remember, Understand, Apply, Analyse, Evaluate, Create. The higher order operations require more complex language, and more complex language develops through higher order thinking. The two are inseparable.

**6. Taking Time for Making Learning Meaningful.** Less content, more depth. Connections to students' lives, interests and prior knowledge. Meaningful learning (Ausubel) transfers and lasts. Surface coverage does not.

**7. Scaffolding Content.** Temporary support structures that reduce cognitive load without reducing cognitive demand. Graphic organisers, visual supports, analogies, chunked tasks, activated prior knowledge. The scaffolding is withdrawn gradually as independence develops.

**8. Scaffolding Language.** The language of the subject requires its own support. Sentence frames, writing frames, modelled phrases, vocabulary organised by function rather than topic. "I think that… because…" The structure externalises the logic of the argument until the learner has internalised it.

**9. Cooperative Learning.** Structured collaboration increases authentic language use, negotiation of meaning, and social safety. The principles of cooperative learning (positive interdependence, individual accountability, promotive interaction) apply fully in CLIL contexts, where the incentive to communicate is also the incentive to learn.

**10. Fostering Learner Autonomy.** Students who can monitor their own progress, choose strategies, and manage their learning are not dependent on a specific teacher or context. CLIL builds toward self-directed learners who can continue beyond the classroom.

**11. Connecting with CLIL Language Speakers and Cultures.** The language of instruction is also a window onto other cultures, communities and ways of knowing. eTwinning, exchanges, authentic contact with speakers: the language becomes a tool of genuine intercultural communication rather than a school subject.

**12. Asymmetry in Classroom Talk in Favour of Students.** The CLIL classroom should feature less presentational talk and more exploratory talk. Students negotiate meaning, build hypotheses, argue, question. The teacher facilitates, models and creates conditions for language to happen. Language is acquired through use.

## The Eight Scaffolding Strategies for Multilingual Learners

(From practice-based research in ELL/multilingual contexts)

**1. Visuals and Realia** — images, objects, real-world items that contextualise vocabulary and concept before text.

**2. Modelling and Gestures** — demonstrating rather than explaining; physical enactment of processes; non-verbal communication as comprehension support.

**3. Connecting to Background Knowledge** — activating prior experience before introducing new content; analogies, questions, images that build a bridge.

**4. Graphic Organisers** — tables, timelines, Venn diagrams, mind maps; visual structures that reduce cognitive load by externalising organisation.

**5. Sentence Structures and Starters** — "The main cause was…", "In comparison with…", "I think… because…"; language frames that allow participation before full linguistic mastery.

**6. Read Aloud** — complex texts read expressively, displayed visually; gives access to grade-level content before independent reading is possible.

**7. Intentional Small Group and Partner Work** — structured collaboration where groupings are chosen for linguistic, cognitive and interpersonal reasons, not convenience.

**8. Use of First Language** — strategic, not full translation; first language as a bridge for key vocabulary and conceptual clarity; identity and comprehension both supported.
