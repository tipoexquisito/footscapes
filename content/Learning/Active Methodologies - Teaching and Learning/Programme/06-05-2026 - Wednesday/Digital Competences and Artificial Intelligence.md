# Notes on Digital Competences and Artificial Intelligence

> "If we teach today as we taught yesterday, we rob our children of tomorrow."
> John Dewey

The session opened on a slide that carried this quote under the heading _From Digital Competences to AI Literacies_. Dewey was not there for decoration. He was an argument: that the question of technology in education is not a technical question. It is a pedagogical one, and it has been the same question for longer than anyone in the room had been teaching.

## The Central Question

"As educators, should we just limit ourselves to adapting our tools to respond to technology — or can we begin to shape the technology itself?"

This was the session's sharpest moment. It arrived early and stayed. Adapting to tools is the path of least resistance and the path most organisations take. Shaping tools requires a different kind of agency, a different relationship to the technology, and a different understanding of what the educator's role actually is. The session did not resolve this. It planted it.

The door and key metaphor organised the rest: which door are you trying to open? Which key do you need? Which lock is stopping you? The metaphor worked because it directed attention toward the barrier before the tool. The question is not "which tool?" The question is "what am I trying to do, for whom, and what is currently making that impossible?"

## DigCompEdu and SELFIE for Teachers

The European framework for educators' digital competence.

[[DigCompEdu]] defines six areas of educator digital competence: Professional Engagement, Digital Resources, Teaching and Learning, Assessment, Empowering Learners, and Facilitating Learners' Digital Competence.

What the session emphasised: DigCompEdu does not assess whether an educator uses tools. It assesses whether the use has pedagogical intent, impact, and equity of access. The distinction between _using technology_ and _using technology purposefully_ runs through every area of the framework.

[[SELFIE for Teachers]] (Self-reflection on Effective Learning by Fostering the use of Innovative Educational Technologies) is the European Commission's self-assessment instrument built on DigCompEdu. Not a test. A mirror.

## The SAMR Model

A framework for thinking about levels of technology integration.

- **Substitution** — technology as a direct replacement for an existing tool, with no functional change.
- **Augmentation** — technology as replacement with some functional improvement.
- **Modification** — technology allows significant task redesign.
- **Redefinition** — technology creates new tasks previously inconceivable.

"We should think of the SAMR model as a spectrum." On one end, a PDF replaces a printed page. On the other end, a student collaborates in real time with a school in another country.

The session was careful about this: "Tools can be used on different levels. It depends on how you use them." A Padlet is not inherently at any level. The same tool becomes substitution or redefinition depending entirely on the pedagogical intention behind it. The tool is not the pedagogy.

## AI Literacy

The session's final turn, and its most open-ended.

"AI literacy represents the technical knowledge, durable skills, and future-ready attitudes required to thrive in a world influenced by AI. It enables learners to engage, create with, manage, and design AI, while critically evaluating its benefits, risks, and ethical implications."

Three things the session insisted on: AI skills differ from ICT and digital skills. They require specialised frameworks. And they are not primarily about tools.

![[048-20260506-group-neda-mirova-digital-competences-session.png]]
*The tutor Neda (BG), English Matter's coordinator Eduardo (ES), and Sílvia (IT) during the Digital Competences session | Day 4 © Team*

The challenges flagged: fairness, transparency, privacy, accountability, and the question of human agency in a world where systems can mimic human behaviour. The UNESCO framework and the OECD draft both treat AI literacy as requiring critical thinking, ethical awareness, and the ability to evaluate rather than simply operate.

PISA 2029 will include media and AI literacy in its assessment. The implication for educators: these are not optional competences for the enthusiasts. They are forming part of the official literacy baseline.

## Station Rotation

Eight tools explored in pairs, ten minutes each.

**Mentimeter** — real-time polling, word clouds, formative feedback.
**NotebookLM** — AI-assisted research and synthesis from uploaded sources.
**Curipod** — AI-generated interactive lesson slides.
**Padlet Arcade** — gamified collaboration. The theme used: [[Guernica]]. The Picasso thread arrived through the back door of a pedagogical tool exercise.
**Webwise** — Irish internet safety centre. Digital citizenship, media literacy, protection resources.
**Genially** — interactive visual content (already encountered in the walking tour). The session closed with a Genially Escape Room in groups of four.
