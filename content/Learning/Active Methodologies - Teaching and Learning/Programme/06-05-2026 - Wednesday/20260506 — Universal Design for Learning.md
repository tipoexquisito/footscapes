# Notes on Universal Design for Learning

The session opened not with a definition but with a question: "What are your barriers?"

Divided deliberately into two: barriers this week, so far, and barriers as a learner in general. The distinction mattered. The second question did not ask about students. It asked about the people in the room. That is where UDL begins: with the recognition that every learner is highly variable, and that the professional in front of a class is not exempt from that variability.

---

## The Definition and the Distinction

"Universal Design for Learning is a set of principles for curriculum development that give all individuals equal opportunities to learn."

The key distinction in the session: UDL is a mindset, not a technique.

At its core, it is not about retrofitting accessible versions of inaccessible lessons. It is about designing for variability from the start, so that the exception becomes the norm and the adaptation is built in rather than added on afterward. The difference between accommodating diversity and designing for it is not semantic. It changes what you make before the lesson exists.

"The UDL approach to teaching minimises barriers and maximises outcomes for all learners."

---

## Three Principles

**Multiple Means of Engagement** — the _why_ of learning. Motivation, interest, belonging, participation, autonomy. Different learners engage differently, and engagement is not a fixed trait of the student. It is a function of the design.

**Multiple Means of Representation** — the _what_ of learning. Text, image, audio, video, diagrams, manipulation, multimodal input. Information presented in one form excludes learners for whom that form is a barrier. Representing content in multiple ways is not redundancy. It is access.

**Multiple Means of Action and Expression** — the _how_ of learning. Oral, written, visual, digital, practical, collaborative. How students demonstrate what they know is as much a design choice as what they are asked to learn.

The session modelled all three. The activities offered multiple entry points. The synthesis task asked participants to choose how to represent their learning: a written note, a voice note, a drawing or comic, a sequence of questions. UDL was enacted as it was being described.

## Three Levels

UDL is not only a classroom framework. It operates across three scales simultaneously.

**Macro level** — directives, legislative acts, national and regional standards and policies. The framework requires systemic adoption, not individual goodwill.

**Meso level** — institutional governance, school policies, community and family links, the conditions for sustaining inclusive innovation over time.

**Micro level** — teaching practices, classroom design, assistive technologies, learning spaces, co-design with learners. This is where most UDL conversations begin, but the session made clear it cannot stay here.

The implication: a teacher who designs for UDL inside an institution that does not support it is working against the current. The question "How could your school improve on their current approach to UDL?" was not rhetorical.

## UDL with Immigrant Students

A specific application that the session addressed directly.

- Place students in mainstream classes and provide support rather than segregate.
- Design inclusive classrooms physically: language labels, visual aids, scaffolded teaching materials.
- Design them emotionally: value home cultures and languages, treat the first language as a resource.
- Use breaking-the-ice activities, peer mentoring, and resources that work for everyone.

The underlying principle: the conditions that support immigrant learners are not separate from good teaching. They are good teaching.

## The Book Pages Activity

The most precise pedagogical move of the session.

Each participant received pages from a text, individually, without context. The task: look at your page. What is happening in the images? How is the man feeling?

Then pairs: look at both pages together and discuss what you think is happening. How do you feel looking at the pages?

Then groups: work together to put the pages into the correct order.

The discomfort of fragmented information without context was the content of the activity. Not an illustration of what learners experience. The actual experience. Understanding followed from feeling, and the metacognitive question that closed the session — "What different forms of representation were used in this series of activities?" — made that explicit: the session had been performing UDL on itself.

## The Goya Puzzle

Before the group left for the afternoon, a Goya painting appeared on screen as a puzzle. _Can you put this painting back together to see this moment in history?_ Then: _What can you see? What do you think is happening?_

The two questions that follow good looking: observation before interpretation. Inference before explanation. The UDL session had been building toward the Prado visit, and the visit had already begun.
