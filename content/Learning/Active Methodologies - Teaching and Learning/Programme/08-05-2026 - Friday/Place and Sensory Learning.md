# Place and Sensory Learning
## Notes on the Morning Session — Friday, 8 May 2026

The last formal session of the week opened on the body before it opened on the city.

The theme was "The role of cognitive science in multisensory learning" — and the first move, characteristically, was not a lecture. It was a question.

---

## Think–Pair–Share: Sensory Memory

"Do you have a particular smell or sound that triggers a memory for you?"

The prompt was an entry point to multisensory learning that arrived through personal experience rather than definition. Before any framework was introduced, the group had already located the argument in themselves: that memory is not primarily textual, that learning leaves traces in the body, that a particular smell or a particular sound can retrieve something the mind has apparently filed elsewhere.

The [[Think-Pair-Share]] structure held it:
- Think: individual, silent.
- Pair: in conversation, testing the experience against someone else's.
- Share: to the group, where the personal became the evidence.

The same cooperative technique had appeared on Tuesday, and again on Wednesday at the Prado. By Friday, participants were not just using it. They were noticing that they were using it again.

---

## Multisensory Learning — The Framework

"Why multisensory approaches overlap with UDL."

The connection is structural, not incidental. UDL's Multiple Means of Representation argues that information presented in one sensory channel excludes learners for whom that channel is a barrier. Multisensory learning approaches the same reality from the cognitive side: the brain encodes more durably through multiple simultaneous channels. Hearing, sight, touch, smell, movement — each adds a strand to the memory trace. Learning designed for multiple senses is not more engaging because it is more entertaining. It is more durable because it is more encoded.

The cognitive science underpinning: sensory memory, emotional engagement, associative learning, autobiographical memory. The modalities that academic education most consistently underuses — smell, touch, kinesthetic, taste — turn out to be among the most emotionally weighted. The classroom that ignores them is not neutral. It is making a choice about which kinds of knowing count.

## Introduction to Toledo — [[Toledo — The City of Three Cultures]]

Before the bus, the city was introduced inside the room.

### The Historical Frame

Toledo's timeline ran from the Roman era through the Visigothic Kingdom, the Moorish conquest, the Christian Reconquista, a Golden Age, and a subsequent decline. The point of the timeline was not chronology. It was convergence: for a period, three traditions — Jewish, Moorish and Christian — occupied the same city simultaneously. The architecture of Toledo is the architecture of that coexistence: a synagogue built in Mudéjar style, a mosque converted to a cathedral, a cathedral built above a mosque.

The Three Cultures framing matters because it refuses the narrative of collision as the default state of cultural difference. Toledo represents a different possibility — not without conflict, but with a record of shared space, shared craft, shared intellectual life.

### Predictive Learning Activity

The session used an image as a predictive prompt: a close-up of figurines of nuns in a shop window. "Can you predict what the story will be about?"

The technique is classic pre-reading scaffolding: activate inference before exposure to content, use a visual cue to build anticipation, make the learner a hypothesis-generator rather than a passive receiver. The image of the nuns also had a later resonance — the shop appeared in Toledo itself, recognised collectively as the group passed it. The session had pre-loaded a moment of shared recognition.

### Wordbuzz / Word Cloud

A word cloud activity followed, tied to the Toledo content. Reading the cloud as a set of associations rather than a sequential text: a different cognitive mode, lateral rather than linear, pattern-recognition rather than comprehension.

### Tableau — Frozen Scene

Pairs read a text about Toledo's history. Each pair defined a gesture for each paragraph. Then: two groups of four, three frozen moments to represent, half the script per group.

The tableau technique — also called "frozen image" or "living picture" — makes comprehension physical. The body has to solve what the mind has processed. Finding the gesture for a paragraph requires understanding it at a level that paraphrase alone does not demand. The collaborative element adds negotiation: the group must agree on what the frozen moment represents, which requires shared interpretation.

The result is learning that has passed through movement, through collective decision, through representation. It leaves a different residue than reading.

## Marzipan Time — Context4Content, Multisensory and Mindful

Before leaving for Toledo, marzipan.

The [[Context4Content]] characteristics named in the session: immersive, outside the comfort zone, fun, engaging. Marzipan is Toledo's traditional confection. The activity brought Toledo into the room through taste before the group had entered the city.

This is a precise methodological move. Multisensory learning argues that memory encodes through multiple channels; tasting Toledo's marzipan before arriving in Toledo creates a sensory anchor that will activate when the same taste appears in a shop, a market stall, a gift. The city becomes associated with a physical memory laid down in advance.

---

## Mindfulness — A Mindful Moment

The session closed with a grounding exercise before the journey.

Three techniques introduced:

**5-4-3-2-1 (Grounding)** — a sensory inventory: five things you can see, four you can feel, three you can hear, two you can smell, one you can taste. The sequence works by pulling attention into the immediate environment, interrupting mental loops by requiring specific sensory observation. The technique also happens to be a structured exercise in exactly the multisensory attention the session had been arguing for.

**Beach Ball Breathing** — a visualisation-guided breathing technique associating breath with the mental image of inflating and releasing a beach ball. Gentle, accessible, non-clinical.

**Box Breathing** — structured breathing in four equal phases: inhale, hold, exhale, hold. Used in stress regulation and focus. The structure is deliberate: it occupies the counting mind while the body regulates.

The invitation carried into Toledo: find time, space, and conscious attention during the afternoon. The city is the classroom. The practice continues.

---

## The Methodology, Briefly

The morning session used, in sequence: Think-Pair-Share, predictive learning, word cloud, tableau, multisensory activation (marzipan), mindfulness grounding. Every technique served the same argument: that learning involves the body, that attention is a teachable skill, and that the most memorable experiences are the ones that engaged the most channels simultaneously.

Toledo was not the destination the session was building toward. It was the continuation of the session by other means.
