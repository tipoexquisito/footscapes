### "What if teachers worked like plumbers?"

A plumber arrives with the right tools, the right training, the right mindset. No household is the same. The leak is a symptom, not the whole problem. Things can be fixed, but only if the diagnosis is honest and the tool is chosen for the job, not applied by habit.

Translated into pedagogy: a CLIL teacher does not apply a fixed sequence to every class. They read the room. They adapt content and language demands simultaneously. They understand that a student struggling with academic language is not struggling with intelligence.

The metaphor holds because it resists the idea of CLIL as a method to be implemented correctly. It positions the teacher as a practitioner who diagnoses, adapts and works with what is in front of them.

The sharpest question of the day came here: "How much unlearning is necessary?"

Every new framework implies something left behind. Every shift in posture requires letting go of a prior certainty. This is uncomfortable. The session did not pretend otherwise, and did not rush past it.

## What Is CLIL

CLIL — Content and Language Integrated Learning — integrates subject content teaching with explicit attention to the language through which that content is learned and expressed.

It is most associated with foreign language instruction but its logic applies anywhere language is the medium through which thinking becomes visible. Which is everywhere.

Two poles in practice:

**Soft CLIL** embeds language support into content teaching without restructuring the lesson around dual objectives. Language development happens through content, but is not foregrounded.

**Hard CLIL** organises the lesson around both content and language objectives from the start. Both are planned for, assessed and treated as equally real learning targets.

The question behind both: who is responsible for language? The session's implicit answer: every teacher is, to some degree, a language teacher. Some do it deliberately. Some do not know they are doing it at all.

## The CLIL Teacher

A CLIL teacher:

- integrates language and content objectives in lesson design
- activates both language and content knowledge at the start of the lesson
- uses multimodal input (visual, oral, written, gestural)
- adapts materials to language level without reducing cognitive demand
- organises structured speaking and writing tasks
- corrects language errors without interrupting cognitive flow
- assesses both subject knowledge and language development
- thinks explicitly about HOTS (Higher-Order Thinking Skills) vs LOTS (Lower-Order Thinking Skills)
- monitors comprehension continuously
- paraphrases and switches registers as needed
- knows subject-specific terminology and teaches it explicitly
- motivates students by making language visible as a tool, not a barrier
- improves language proficiency as a by-product of subject teaching

The tension in practice: content teachers trained in a subject discipline are not always trained in language observation. Becoming a CLIL teacher requires adding a layer of attention that was never part of the original role. That is the unlearning the session was pointing at.

## Scaffolding

The central technical concept of the session.

"Providing students with temporary supportive structures gradually removed as independence develops." (Riddett, 2015; Gibbons, 2002)

The purpose: "Lowering the barriers to comprehension."

The principle: everyone reaches equivalent outcomes. What varies is the level and type of scaffolding, and the pace at which it is withdrawn. This is not differentiation as separate tracks. It is differentiation as equitable access to the same destination.

[[Scaffolding]] is not simplification. Simplifying content reduces cognitive demand. Scaffolding maintains cognitive demand and changes the conditions of access.

This is a precise and important distinction. A scaffolded task is harder to design than a simplified one. It asks the teacher to hold two things simultaneously: the rigour of the learning objective and the specific barrier a particular learner faces.

## BICS and CALP

[[BICS and CALP]]: A distinction worth carrying beyond language classrooms.

**BICS** — Basic Interpersonal Communication Skills: the conversational fluency students develop in informal social contexts. Fast to acquire (two to three years). Can mask underlying gaps in academic language.

**CALP** — Cognitive Academic Language Proficiency: the register required for academic tasks, argumentation, abstract thinking and discipline-specific vocabulary. Takes significantly longer to develop (five to seven years in a second language context).

A student who sounds fluent may still be operating without the cognitive-academic register the subject requires. This matters in every content classroom where fluency is assumed and academic struggle is misread as a comprehension failure.

## PREP

A practical scaffolding structure for oral argumentation. Simple enough to teach in ten minutes. Flexible enough to adapt across subjects and levels.

**Point** — state your position
> In my opinion / I believe / I reckon / I think / If you ask me...

**Reason** — explain why
> ...because...

**Example** — provide evidence or illustration

**Point** — restate your position as conclusion

The structure works because it externalises the logic of argument. Students who struggle with academic oral production often have the thinking but not the language to organise it. PREP provides the scaffolding without replacing the thought.

## The Jigsaw

[[Jigsaw]] is a cooperative structure adapted for language tasks. Each student or group receives a different piece of the text or content. Understanding the whole requires exchange. Language is the tool of that exchange. The design guarantees that everyone speaks, because everyone holds information the others need.

## One Question Worth Keeping

"Who are your students' other teachers?"

The question points to something systemic. A CLIL approach does not happen in one lesson or one teacher's practice. Language development is cumulative, cross-disciplinary and social. What one teacher builds, another can dismantle. The question is whether those teachers know what each other is doing. Usually, they do not.
