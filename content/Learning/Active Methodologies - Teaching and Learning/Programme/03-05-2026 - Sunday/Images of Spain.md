# Notes on Images of Spain

> How did the Spanish become who they are?

The session moved chronologically from Altamira to 2026. Cave paintings from 35,000 BCE. Phoenician gold from Seville. Roman Hispania with its aqueduct at Segovia and its theatre at Mérida, and the quiet detail that Seneca, Trajan, Hadrian and Marcus Aurelius all came from the peninsula. The Visigoths. Toledo as capital. Al-Andalus. The Mosque of Córdoba. The Alhambra. The Reconquista mapped in four stages across four centuries. The Catholic Monarchs. Felipe II and the empire that reached the Philippines. Carlos III, described as the best mayor Madrid ever had, who built primary schools and decreed all professions honourable. The Civil War. Franco. 1978.

The selection was panoramic and largely institutional. A national narrative, coherent and legible, oriented toward heritage, continuity and artistic legacy. What it did not hold much space for were the tensions inside that chronology: the colonial shadow of the empire, the violence of the Inquisition, the expulsion of Jews and Muslims in 1492, the complexity of what _reconquista_ means depending on who is telling the story.

And yet the throughline that gave the session its pedagogical force was precisely coexistence. Toledo as Visigothic capital before it became the city of three cultures. Córdoba as the intellectual centre of medieval Europe under Moorish rule. The Alhambra not as interruption but as foundation. The session showed, almost despite itself, that Spain's identity is hybrid, layered and built from contact as much as from conflict.

The session closed with Federico García Lorca's _Canción de Jinete_, a poem that carries all of that weight in a few lines. Córdoba. Far away and lonely. It was a good choice. History as image, as sound, as felt experience. [[Heritage as Curriculum]] in practice, before the term had been introduced.

This is [[Context4Content]] working at the level of historical narrative. The monument as document. The poem as evidence. The past as a resource for making sense of the present.
