The day closed with a quiz about Spain.

It was also an icebreaker. It was also [[Game-Based Learning]] before anyone called it that. The point was never the answers. It was the conversation around them, the laughter, the surprise, the first moments of a group becoming a group. Twelve tables. Nine countries. Twenty-eight participants who had arrived as strangers and were leaving as something more than that.

The quiz did something else quietly. It extended the [[Images of Spain]] session into a participatory mode. Knowledge that had been received became knowledge negotiated, tested, debated across a table. That shift, from reception to interaction, from audience to participant, was itself a small demonstration of the active methodology the week would spend seven days exploring.

Structure creates connection. Connection creates community. Community creates the conditions for learning.

---

One answer from the quiz is worth carrying forward.

Among the questions about Spanish geography and culture, a painting appeared: _El 3 de Mayo de 1808 en Madrid_, [[Goya|Francisco de Goya y Lucientes]]. The facilitator offered a framing that did not come from an art history textbook: Goya not as court painter — which he also was — but as war reporter. Someone who stayed in the city during the French occupation. Who looked at close range. Who painted, six years later, what he had witnessed: an execution, at night, on the outskirts of Madrid, ordinary people in front of rifles they did not choose.

It was 3 May 2026. In Madrid.

The coincidence was not announced. It did not need to be. But the weight of that date, and that painting, in that city, carried something no slide could have planned for. _History does not repeat itself. It rhymes._

That image would return. At the [[Highlights of Spanish Art|Prado]], later in the week. In the conversations about art as testimony that kept surfacing in unexpected places. In [[Fiat Humanitas]], the final note of the whole journey. Goya was the first of several artists the programme would use, deliberately or not, to anchor pedagogical arguments in specific human moments. [[Picasso]] would follow. So would [[Hieronymus Bosch]]. So would [[Diego Velázquez]]. Art as document. As moral witness. As the kind of evidence that stays long after the quiz answer is forgotten.
