## What It Shows

The young Philip IV of Spain in formal court dress. One of several portraits [[Diego Velázquez|Velázquez]] painted of the king across decades, this early work establishes the visual language of royal self-presentation: erect posture, controlled expression, the body used as an instrument of power.

---

## Pose as Political Language

The portrait is not a record of a person. It is a construction of a role.

The king's posture, dress, and expression are designed to communicate authority, stability, and legitimacy to anyone who encounters the image — whether in the palace, in reproduction, or in diplomatic contexts. The individual is subsumed into the office.

This is official representation at its most deliberate: the image does not show how the king is, but how power requires the king to appear.

---

## Connections in the Course

The gap between the person and the role connects to ongoing discussions of **representation and framing**: whose image circulates, who controls its production, and what interests that image serves. See [[Las Meninas]], [[La familia de Carlos IV]], and [[What is truth in 2026?]].

The portrait tradition also provides a historical anchor for contemporary questions about **digital self-presentation** and the politics of the profile image.
