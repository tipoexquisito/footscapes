## The Character

Don Quixote de la Mancha is a minor nobleman who has read too many chivalric romances and decides to become a knight-errant. He misreads reality — systematically, persistently, magnificently. Windmills are giants. Inns are castles. A peasant woman is a noblewoman. He acts on the world as he imagines it, not as it is.

This is not simply madness. It is a philosophical position: that imagination has its own kind of truth, and that the world we inhabit is partly the world we choose to see.

---

## The Transformation

The relationship between Don Quixote and his squire [[Sancho Panza]] is the novel's moral engine. Sancho begins grounded, pragmatic, sceptical. Quixote begins idealistic, deluded, relentless. By the end, each has become something of the other.

This mutual transformation — the way learning changes both parties — connects directly to the teacher-learner relationship. The facilitator who never changes is not facilitating. They are performing.

---

## Don Quixote as First Modern Novel

Cervantes built a text that is aware of its own fictionality, that includes readers as characters, that questions the reliability of its own narrators. It was doing in 1605 what postmodern literature claims as recent innovation.

It is also, among other things, a meditation on how stories shape behaviour — how the narratives we consume become the lenses through which we see possibility.
