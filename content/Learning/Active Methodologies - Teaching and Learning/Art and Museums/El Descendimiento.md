## What It Shows

The body of Christ being lowered from the cross. Ten figures: disciples, women, the Virgin. The composition is compressed into a shallow space, almost theatrical — as if the scene were staged against a flat backdrop rather than set in depth.

---

## Emotion as Cognitive Tool

[[Rogier van der Weyden|Van der Weyden]]'s central technique is the transfer of grief.

Each figure in the painting responds to the same event with a different emotional register: prostrate collapse (the Virgin), silent shock, active service, restrained dignity. The viewer reads across a spectrum of responses to loss.

This is not decoration. It is a structured emotional argument: grief is real, it is shared, it takes different forms in different bodies, and to witness it fully you must inhabit more than one perspective at once.

---

## Connections in the Course

The painting raises the question of **empathy as a learning condition**. Learning that has no affective dimension tends not to stay. What you feel, you remember. See [[Carl Rogers — Experiential Learning]] and [[Why use a multi-sensory approach?]].

The staged, almost choreographed composition also connects to **cooperative learning**: different roles, different positions, a shared task — and the whole only visible when you hold all perspectives simultaneously.
