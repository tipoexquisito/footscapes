## What It Is

"African Guernica" is the name given to a body of work by South African artist [[Dumile Feni]], created under apartheid. The title is not a comparison — it is a proposal for a trans-historical reading of violence.

> "History doesn't repeat itself, it does rhyme."

---

## The Post-Colonial Displacement

The exhibition shifts the centre of the European narrative.

The suffering represented by [[Picasso]] in [[Guernica]] stops being exclusively European. It enters into dialogue with colonialism, apartheid, structural racism, displacement, exclusion, and systemic violence. Geographies and systems of power vary; the grammar of violence does not.

---

## The Body as Political Archive

As in Guernica, the bodies in Dumile Feni's work appear distorted, wounded, fragmented, spectral. The body stops being an anatomical representation and becomes a historical archive — a record of what power does to people over time.

---

## The Reconfiguration of the Canon

The exhibition poses a quiet but serious question: who enters the history of art? Whose suffering receives institutional recognition? Whose universalism is assumed, and whose must be argued for?

Placing African Guernica in dialogue with Picasso does not diminish either work. It expands the concept of cultural memory — showing that the violence Guernica testified to did not end in 1937, and did not stay in Europe.
