Squire to [[Don Quixote]] in [[Cervantes]]'s novel. The grounded, practical, self-interested counterweight to Quixote's idealism — and, by the end, something much more than that.

---

## The Character

Sancho begins as a peasant farmer who joins Quixote expecting material reward: a governorship, an island, advancement for his family. He is suspicious of abstractions, attentive to his stomach, loyal in a matter-of-fact way that has nothing to do with romance.

His speech is full of proverbs. He carries the accumulated wisdom of ordinary life and is not ashamed of it.

---

## The Transformation

The novel's central moral movement is the slow convergence between Quixote and Sancho. The idealist learns something of pragmatism; the pragmatist learns something of vision. By the final chapters, Sancho is arguing for the chivalric project his master has abandoned.

This exchange — two people genuinely changed by sustained contact — is a model of what learning relationships, at their best, produce. Neither party exits unchanged.
