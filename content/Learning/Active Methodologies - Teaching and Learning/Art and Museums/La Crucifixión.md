## What It Shows

Christ on the cross, set against a dark and turbulent sky. The figure is elongated in [[El Greco]]'s characteristic manner — beyond anatomical proportion, toward spiritual intensity. The surrounding figures (Mary, John, Mary Magdalene) respond with contained grief.

---

## Representation Is Not Reproduction

El Greco's bodies do not follow the rules of classical anatomy. The proportions are deliberately distorted, the light is not natural, the space is not perspectivally coherent.

And yet the painting is immediately, viscerally legible. The distortion serves the truth of the experience: the elongation of the body communicates vertical aspiration, elevation, suffering that exceeds the physical.

This is one of the clearest demonstrations in the Prado that **representation is not the same as reproduction**. The painter's choices — which rules to follow, which to break — are arguments about what is most real.

---

## Connections in the Course

The gap between anatomical accuracy and expressive truth connects to **teaching practice**: transmitting information faithfully is not the same as producing understanding. What we omit, distort, or emphasise in a lesson is always a set of decisions about what matters most.

See [[Active Methodologies]], [[Curation]], and the recurring discussion of what it means to design a learning experience rather than simply deliver content.
