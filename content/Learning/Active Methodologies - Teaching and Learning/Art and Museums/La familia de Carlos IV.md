## What It Shows

The royal family of Charles IV, posed in formal portrait. Fourteen figures. [[Goya]] himself appears in the background at the left, brush in hand — a deliberate echo of [[Las Meninas]].

---

## The Question Underneath the Surface

The painting is technically dazzling and quietly subversive.

Official royal portraiture was an instrument of legitimacy: the family rendered radiant, powerful, unified. Goya complies with the commission — and does something else. The figures are rendered with unflinching realism: the King soft and slightly vacant, the Queen dominant and forward-thrusting, the children candid and unposed. There is no idealisation.

Whether Goya intended this as critique or simply as truthful observation is a question the painting refuses to answer.

---

## Representation and Power

The work opens a conversation about who commissions images, what images are expected to say, and the distance between intended meaning and received meaning. Official representation is always a negotiation between power and the one given the tools to record it.

This connects to [[Las Meninas]], [[AI Literacy]], and [[What is truth in 2026?]] — the recurring question of who frames the image and what the frame conceals.
