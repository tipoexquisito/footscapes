Thematic itinerary on the upper floor of the [[Museo Nacional Centro de Arte Reina Sofia]]. Archive, identity, public space, and critical consciousness.

---

## From Representation to Interrogation

After 1975, art stops trying primarily to represent the world. It tries to interrogate systems — political, mediatic, identitary, colonial, economic, institutional.

The work changes form: installation, video, archive, performance, document, spatial intervention. The single object gives way to environments that demand active reading.

---

## The Visitor's Role Changes

The visitor stops being a contemplative observer and becomes interpreter, participant, critical reader, agent of meaning-making.

Contemporary art rarely provides a single reading. It requires negotiation, inference, contextualisation, and historical awareness. The museum stops being a place of answers and becomes a space of productive difficulty.

---

## Marginalised Voices, Silenced Memories

One of the strongest threads across this floor: the emergence of voices that had been absent from the canon — marginalised identities, hybrid subjectivities, peripheral memories, silenced communities.

The central question is quiet but persistent: *who has the right to representation?*

This connects directly to [[African Guernica]] by [[Dumile Feni]], seen in the same visit — a work that asks the same question from a different continent and a different system of violence.

---

## Art, Public Space, and Political Consciousness

Many works here extend beyond the museum into protest, social archive, media, activism, urbanism, and public sphere. Contemporary art understood in this way is not decoration — it is a form of civic participation.

---

## The Cultural Broth

The most lasting impression from the floor: every work participates in a historical ecology of meanings. Nothing exists in isolation. Each work carries political debates, visual languages, collective memories, social conflicts, cultural imaginaries, communication technologies, urban transformations, and shifts in sensibility.

To read contemporary art is to read the world it came from — and to recognise how much of that world is still present.
