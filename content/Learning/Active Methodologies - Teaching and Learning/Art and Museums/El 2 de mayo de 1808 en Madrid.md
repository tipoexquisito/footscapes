## What It Shows

The painting depicts the popular uprising in Madrid on 2 May 1808, when the city's residents attacked Mamluk cavalry serving the French occupying forces. [[Goya]] painted it six years after the event, not as a witness but as a moral interpreter.

There is no stable centre. The composition is deliberately chaotic: diagonal thrusts, overlapping bodies, horses rearing, weapons clashing at close range. The scene resists the eye's search for order — and that resistance is the point.

---

## Composition as Argument

Classical battle painting placed a hero at the centre: identifiable, commanding, triumphant. Goya refuses this entirely.

The figures are anonymous. No uniform identifies a leader; no face is elevated above the rest. Resistance is collective, unnamed, improvised. The violence is immediate and unglamorous.

This is the visual logic of testimony rather than commemoration.

---

## The Diptych

El 2 de mayo was painted as the companion piece to [[El 3 de mayo en Madrid]]. Together they form a single moral argument about war — the uprising and its consequence. Chaos and execution. Resistance and retribution.

Goya does not celebrate either. He witnesses both.

## In the Context of the Course

The painting connects to several threads running through the week:

The question of **representation** — who gets to name events, how power constructs its version of history — runs from here directly to [[Las Meninas]], [[Guernica]] and [[What is truth in 2026?]].

The **collective, anonymous figure** connects to [[Universal Design for Learning]]: learning designed for everyone, not centred on the exceptional individual.

The chaotic composition mirrors the pedagogical value of **productive difficulty** — the discomfort that precedes understanding.
