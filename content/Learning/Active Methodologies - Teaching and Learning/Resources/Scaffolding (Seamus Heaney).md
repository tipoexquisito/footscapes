[[Seamus Heaney]], from *Door into the Dark*, 1969.

---

Masons, when they start upon a building,
Are careful to test out the scaffolding;

Make sure that planks won't slip at busy points,
Secure all ladders, tighten bolted joints.

And yet all this comes down when the job's done
Flung on a truck, jobbed off to be used again.

So if, my dear, there's something at a loss
Between our two loves, the loss is gain.

Build it all up again, and when the structure's raised,
Take it down and start again.

---

## The Pedagogical Reading

Heaney's poem carries a double metaphor. The explicit subject is a love relationship — built up, taken down, rebuilt. But the poem is equally a description of good pedagogical practice.

Scaffolding is temporary by design. It is not the building — it is what makes the building possible. The goal of every scaffold is its own removal: when the learner no longer needs the support, the scaffold has succeeded.

The poem reminds us that this removal is not a loss. It is the moment the structure stands on its own.
