The presentation was designed as a visual and sensory argument, not just a slide deck.

Each slide is anchored in a painting seen that week at the Prado. The works don't illustrate the concepts — they carry them. The transition from slide 1 to slide 2 is a zoom-out of [[Las Meninas]]: a detail becomes a whole composition, asking the viewer to step back and see the bigger picture. It was Sofia, the Prado guide, who had said exactly that — different vantage points change everything you think you understand about a painting, a classroom, a student. The zoom-out is her argument made visible.

Between slides, the frames that surround each painting extend beyond the image — lines suggesting walls, ceiling, floor, vanishing points. Perspective lines as architecture. The presentation itself practises what it argues: that the way you frame something determines what you can see inside it.

The final moment is a multisensory activity. The audience is asked to produce a sound based on an image they are about to see. The image is a green pedestrian traffic light — the one found throughout Madrid, with its distinctive _piu piu piu_ of small birds. The group reacted before they understood why. That is [[Multisensory Learning|synaesthesia]] in practice — the same principle introduced during the [[Place and Sensory Learning]] session the morning before. The presentation did not explain the concept. It activated it.
