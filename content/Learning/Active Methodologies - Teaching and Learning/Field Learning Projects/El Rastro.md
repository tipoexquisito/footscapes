_Sunday, 3 May 2026. La Latina, Madrid._

The course had not yet begun. The Portuguese group had found each other the night before, the way Erasmus+ groups always do, through a message thread and a vague plan for breakfast. Sunday morning brought _pan con tomate_ at La Latina and then, naturally, El Rastro.

El Rastro is Madrid's oldest and largest street market, running every Sunday through the streets below La Latina. It is loud, dense and entirely uncurated. Stalls selling second-hand tools, vinyl records, vintage clothing, ceramics, phone cases and objects of uncertain origin stretch down Calle de la Ribera de Curtidores and spill into the surrounding streets. The crowd is a mix of locals, tourists, collectors and people who simply came for the walk.

![[002-20260503-andre-el-rastro.png]]
*El Rastro, Sunday morning | Day 1 © André Almeida*

Nobody planned a lesson. But the learning started immediately.

The market created conditions for exactly the kind of language use that [[CLIL]] and [[Scaffolding]] theory describe but rarely gets to observe in its natural state. Prices negotiated in Spanish by non-speakers. Greetings exchanged through gesture when words ran out. Signs read and misread. Directions asked and partially understood. The communicative pressure was real, low-stakes and productive. This is what [[BICS and CALP#BICS|BICS]] looks like when it is actually working: informal, spontaneous, embedded in context, driven by genuine need.

But El Rastro also offered something less visible. Walking through a Sunday market in a city that is not your own requires a particular kind of attention. You read the space differently. You notice what is sold and what that reveals about who lives here. You notice the informal economy, the sustainability logic of reuse, the way heritage and everyday life share the same street without ceremony. That is [[BICS and CALP#CALP|CALP]] territory: intercultural competence, urban literacy, critical observation of public space.

The market sits in one of Madrid's oldest neighbourhoods. La Latina carries layers of history that the walk surfaced without announcing them. The cobblestones, the medieval street pattern, the churches alongside the market stalls. [[Context4Content]]  before the term had been introduced. The city as document, the street as curriculum.

There is something worth noting about beginning this way, informally, without structure, before the programme started. [[Place-based Learning]] does not require a task card. It requires presence and attention. El Rastro offered both, freely, on a Sunday morning.

The _pan con tomate_ was excellent.
