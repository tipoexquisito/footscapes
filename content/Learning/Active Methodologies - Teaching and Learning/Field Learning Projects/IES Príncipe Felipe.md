## Day note synthesis

The school that had the week in its bones without knowing it.

[[IES Príncipe Felipe]] is a hybrid institution: secondary education, vocational training, artistic programmes, bilingual teaching, Bachillerato Internacional, and Erasmus+ placements, all under one roof. It was established in its current form in 1997, in Fuencarral–El Pardo, a socially heterogeneous neighbourhood that makes the school's official discourse on inclusion legible against a real context rather than an aspirational one.

The visit moved through corridors and laboratories. Television production. Makeup and aesthetics. Social care. Audiovisual. The labs were not workshops appended to a real curriculum. They were the curriculum made spatial. Each room declared something about what the school considered worth learning: image-making, human care, body, craft, mediation. Competencies that Portuguese vocational education often treats as peripheral, here presented as central to the school's identity.

The director did not speak English. The language coordinator mediated. What could have been an awkward administrative moment became, instead, a live demonstration of what the week had been teaching: that CLIL is not about anglicisation. It is about negotiated meaning, shared across language difference, carried by whoever in the room has the right bridge.

The "alumnos wifi" appeared in conversation: student volunteers who manage the school's digital infrastructure, troubleshoot in classrooms, and support the technology the institution runs on. Not passive users. Co-managers. The phrase carried an entire pedagogy in two words.

Erasmus+ was not a programme the school ran. It was part of how the school described itself. The institutional text reads: "todos nuestros estudiantes deberían de tener una oportunidad europea." Europe as pedagogical project, not administrative category.

The comparison with Portugal was not made out loud. But it was present in every room. The FP visible, equipped, proud, internationalised. Not marginal.

# IES Príncipe Felipe
## School Visit — Thursday, 7 May 2026

A school that was built on the same arguments the week had been making, without having attended the same course.

The IES Príncipe Felipe is a public secondary and vocational institution in Fuencarral–El Pardo, a socially heterogeneous district in north Madrid. It was formed in 1997 through the merger of a bachillerato institute and a vocational training centre. That origin is not a historical footnote. It is the institution's architectural logic: the school has never treated academic and vocational education as separate tracks. They coexist under one roof, sharing infrastructure, culture and, critically, the same claim to legitimacy.

## The Hybrid Model

The IES offers, simultaneously:

- Secondary education (ESO and Bachillerato)
- Bachillerato Internacional
- Formación Profesional across multiple families: audiovisual production, aesthetics and beauty, social and community services, administration and finance
- Bilingual teaching programme (from 2014/15)
- Erasmus+ institutional programme
- Innovation in Technology designation (from 2010/11)

What this means in practice: a student at the IES can move between an academic trajectory and a vocational one without changing schools, without crossing a symbolic boundary that in most European systems marks the difference between education for thinking and education for doing. The IES refuses that distinction structurally.

## Pedagogia Espacializada — The Labs as Curriculum

The visit moved through specialist laboratories. Television production. Makeup, aesthetics and hairdressing. Social integration and early childhood education. Audiovisual production and lighting. Administration.

These rooms are not ancillary. They are the argument.

Each lab encodes something about what the school considers worth learning. Image-making, human care, the body, craft, technical mediation, social support: competencies that academic tradition has systematically undervalued, here treated as the core of a professional education. The space itself teaches. Students enter a lab and their posture changes, their language changes, their relationship to the task changes. This is what the working notes called "pedagogia espacializada": the curriculum written into architecture.

![[055-20260507-group-school-presentation-ies-principe-felipe.png]]
*School presentation, IES Príncipe Felipe | Day 5 © Team*

The connection to [[Universal Design for Learning]] is direct. Multiple Means of Action and Expression are not an add-on in this building. They are the building. Students demonstrate what they know by making things, caring for people, managing equipment, producing images. The assessment follows the mode of knowing rather than forcing every mode of knowing through the same assessment funnel.

## The Director and the Coordinator — CLIL in the Real World

The director of the school did not speak English.

![[056-20260507-filipe-ies-principe-felipe-coordinator.png]]
*IES Príncipe Felipe language's coordinator | Day 5 © Filipe Gama*

In a week that had devoted considerable time to CLIL — to scaffolding, to language as a cognitive tool, to the BICS/CALP distinction — this was an unexpectedly precise illustration. The language coordinator stepped in as bridge. The conversation continued, multilingual, negotiated, functional.

This is what CLIL looks like outside the idealised classroom: content — in this case, the school's programmes, its philosophy, its history — crossed by language difference, managed collectively, with whoever holds the linguistic bridge taking a mediating role. No one suggested the director's lack of English disqualified the school's bilingual programme. The point was precisely that bilingualism and internationalisation are not the same thing, and that one can build a genuinely European institution without requiring every person in it to perform the same linguistic competence.

## "Alumnos Wifi"

The school designates a group of student volunteers as "alumnos wifi."

They manage classroom technology, troubleshoot digital infrastructure, and provide technical support to teaching staff and to other students. The role is voluntary, formally recognised, and integrated into the school's technological identity.

![[065-20260507-group-filipe-testing-desk-ies-principe-felipe.png]]
*Filipe (PT) testing a computer desk, IES Príncipe Felipe | Day 5 © Team*

The pedagogical significance is exact: learner autonomy taken to its institutional limit. These students are not receiving instruction in digital competences. They are exercising them as agents within the institution. The school treats student expertise as a resource rather than an exception. The "alumnos wifi" are a form of peer mentoring, a form of cooperative learning, and a form of active methodology, operating simultaneously, without any of those labels being invoked.

## Erasmus+ as Institutional Identity

The school does not run an Erasmus+ programme. It identifies itself through one.

The institutional documentation is explicit: "todos nuestros estudiantes deberían de tener una oportunidad europea." The sentence frames European mobility not as an optional enhancement for high-achieving students but as something every student at the school is entitled to access.

![[062-20260507-filipe-student-photos-erasmus-ies-principe-felipe.png]]
*An exhibition of students' Erasmus+ experience pictures, IES Príncipe Felipe | Day 5 © Filipe Gama*

For vocational students, this takes a specific form: placements in European companies, not exchanges to European universities. The professional trajectory is internationalised directly. A student in audiovisual production, aesthetics or social care completes a work placement somewhere in Europe. The Erasmus+ Spirit the week had discussed — as philosophical disposition, as recognition of diversity, as democratic commitment — is here an operational component of a vocational qualification.

## The Prado and the Audiovisual Lab — A Thread Completed

In the working notes from the visit, this connection was named directly: [[Las Meninas]] and the audiovisual laboratory are asking the same questions.

![[058-20260507-group-las-meninas-interpretation-ies-principe-felipe.png]]
*A "Las Meninas" artwork interpretation spotted at IES Príncipe Felipe | Day 5 © Team*

Las Meninas asks: Who is watching? Who is being watched? What is happening outside the frame? What does the mirror show? Who is the painter painting?

![[059-20260507-group-team-resting-tv-studio-ies-principe-felipe.png]]
*Maria Grazia (IT), Claudia (IT), Mara (IT), Carsten (DE) and Megan (UK) in the TV set at IES Príncipe Felipe | Day 5 © Team*

![[067-20260507-filipe-media-control-room-ies-principe-felipe.png]]
*The media control room, IES Príncipe Felipe | Day 5 © Filipe Gama*

The audiovisual lab asks: Where do you place the camera? What does the frame include and exclude? Who controls the image? How is the viewer positioned? What does the light do to what it illuminates?

These are not analogous questions. They are the same question, asked in different centuries with different tools.

[[Goya]]'s paintings — the 2 and 3 of May, the Pinturas Negras, the portraits of the royal family — raise questions about representation, narrative, propaganda and the construction of reality through visual means. The audiovisual curriculum at the IES is, among other things, a technical education in exactly those operations. Media literacy and visual literacy are not separate subjects that happen to share vocabulary. They are one project running across several centuries.

## The PT/ES Comparison — Unsaid but Present

The comparison between Portuguese and Spanish vocational education was not part of the official visit agenda. It was present in every room.

In Spain, the Formación Profesional has institutional visibility, dedicated infrastructure, social legitimacy, and a direct path to European mobility. The IES presents its FP cycles as central to its identity, not as an alternative for students who didn't make it elsewhere.

In Portugal, vocational education has been expanding, but the symbolic hierarchy — academic over vocational, university over everything else — remains largely intact. A student in a Curso Profissional in Portugal does not typically encounter a school that describes itself through that course as its primary institutional face.

![[057-20260507-group-visit-ies-principe-felipe.png]]
*Megan (UK) highlighting differences in European education systems, IES Príncipe Felipe | Day 5 © Team*

This is not a critique. It is an observation the visit made available, quietly, through contrast.

## Synthesis

The IES Príncipe Felipe functioned on Thursday morning as the week's most comprehensive test case.

![[061-20260507-filipe-student-locker-ies-principe-felipe.png]]
*Student locker customisation culture, IES Príncipe Felipe | Day 5 © Filipe Gama*

Every methodological principle the sessions had introduced was either visible in the school's practice or illuminated by its absence. UDL in the multimodal labs. CLIL in the negotiated encounter at the director's door. Cooperative learning in the "alumnos wifi" programme. Active methodologies in rooms that made passivity architecturally difficult. The European Dimension in a school that sends vocational students to European companies and means it.

![[063-20260507-filipe-another-student-locker-ies-principe-felipe.png]]
*Another student locker, IES Príncipe Felipe | Day 5 © Filipe Gama*

The school did not illustrate the course. It extended it. The morning showed that methodology without institutional culture, without infrastructure, without symbolic legitimacy, is a set of techniques suspended in mid-air. The IES had built the air into a building.

![[064-20260507-filipe-yet-another-student-locker-ies-principe-felipe.png]]
*Yet another student locker, IES Príncipe Felipe | Day 5 © Filipe Gama*
