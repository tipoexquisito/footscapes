> At four in the afternoon, the classroom disappeared.


By afternoon, the argument left the room entirely and took the group into the city, where Madrid stopped being a backdrop and became the curriculum itself. The streets, the monuments, the food, the movement through urban space: all of it functioning as content, as context, as learning environment.

Among the locations: the Royal Palace, and a plaque. One of the quiz questions asked what had happened there on 2 May 1808. The answer pointed directly to [[Goya]], to the companion paintings that would be waiting at the [[Highlights of Spanish Art|Prado]] later in the week, and to the thread of art as testimony that was already running, quietly, through the whole programme.

The day's underlying logic can be read as a single arc:

From the educator's identity, to the competences that shape learning, to the city as the space where both are tested in real conditions.

---

For [[Images of Madrid - a walking tour]], the group split into two and moved into the city with phones, a Seesaw app, a Genially map and a quiz structure built around eight locations. The activity lasted ninety minutes, officially. In practice, it lasted as long as anyone wanted it to.

What the structure did not announce was how pedagogically complete it was. The walking tour combined a scavenger hunt, [[Inquiry-Based Learning]], game-based elements, [[Collaborative Learning]] and digital documentation, without any of those labels being visible to participants. The gamification was light: one point per photo, one per correct answer, bonus points for creativity. Enough to structure attention and motivate movement, not enough to override curiosity.

The route itself was a curated argument about Madrid. Plaza Mayor, Restaurante Botín, the Royal Palace, Plaza de Oriente, Puerta del Sol. Each location carried a question that required active observation. Which parts of the bear are gold? How did Galileo solve the structural problem of the rearing horse? What happened here on 2 May 1808?

This last question was not incidental. The Royal Palace plaque commemorating the 2nd of May connects directly to [[Goya]], to the works that would appear later in the week at the Prado, and to the political and cultural threads that run through the whole programme. The city was already reading itself as curriculum before anyone named it that way.

The [[Genially]] map transformed Madrid into an interactive interface, giving the tour a non-linear quality. Participants could approach locations in different orders, make different decisions, notice different things. The [[Seesaw]] platform collected the results as a shared portfolio: photos, answers and creative responses that became a collective memory of the afternoon.

What made the activity genuinely rich was the pedagogy of observation it demanded. Not passing through. Looking. Reading the golden parts of a statue. Finding a plaque on the ground. Working out a structural solution from visual evidence. The city rewarded attention, and that is itself a [[Place-based Learning]] argument: that environments teach when learners are asked to read them carefully.

There is a question worth holding, even so. The narrative Madrid that the route presented was monumental, imperial and largely celebratory. The buildings are real. The history they carry is also real, and more contested than a quiz can hold. What the tour could not quite accommodate was the other Madrid: the social tensions, the colonial histories embedded in those same streets, the questions a more critical urban literacy would ask. That is not an argument against the activity. It is an argument for what might follow it.

The afternoon ended, with cañas and tapas, and a few countries sitting around a table comparing notes on what they had seen.
