---

## What Curation Is

A museum curator does not simply collect objects. They decide which objects to place in proximity, in what order, with what lighting, what wall text, what silences between them. The meaning emerges from the arrangement, not from any single piece.

Teaching works the same way.

The teacher who curates a learning experience is not deciding *what* to tell learners. They are deciding *how to arrange the conditions* under which learners will encounter something worth understanding.

---

## The Hidden Methodology

The week's deepest methodology was curation. The explicit frameworks — [[Active Methodologies]], [[Cooperative Learning]], [[CLIL]], [[Universal Design for Learning]] — were the tools. Curation was the sensibility that held them together.

Which activities in which order. Which museum first. Which conversation before which artwork. Which silence after which film. These are curatorial decisions, and they shape whether the experience coheres or merely accumulates.

---

## The Teacher as Curator

Curation as a pedagogical stance implies several commitments:

The learning environment is designed with intention, not assembled by default. Learner experience — not content coverage — is the primary design criterion. The sequence and rhythm of activities matter as much as the activities themselves. The gaps between things are as important as the things.
