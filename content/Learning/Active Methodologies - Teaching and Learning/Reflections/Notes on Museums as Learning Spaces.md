A museum is not a repository. Treated properly, it is a classroom that happens to be full of evidence.

The difference between a visit and a lesson is preparation. At the Centro de Arte Oliva, the route was walked in advance, the questions were designed before the students arrived, and a guided tour became something closer to an inquiry: what do you think this means, asked once at the entrance and again at the exit, an hour apart, with the answers changing completely in between. That single repeated question did more pedagogical work than any worksheet could have. [[Context4Content]] names the principle; the museum supplied the proof.

This is [[Place-based Learning|place-based learning]] applied to an institution built for exactly this purpose. Museums already curate, sequence and juxtapose — the raw material of a lesson is done before a teacher arrives. What a teacher adds is the question that turns a room of objects into an argument, and the follow-up that turns a good answer into a better one.

[[Visual Learning|Visual learning]] runs underneath all of it. A painting, a photograph, an installation: each demands a kind of attention that text does not, slower, more circular, willing to sit with ambiguity before resolving it. Museums as learning spaces work because they insist on that slowness, in an educational culture that increasingly does not.
