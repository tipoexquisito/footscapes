## The Starting Point

> "The great enemy of the truth is very often not the lie — deliberate, contrived and dishonest — but the myth — persistent, persuasive and unrealistic." — John F. Kennedy, 1962

Kennedy's distinction is more useful now than when it was made. The problem of 2026 is not primarily deliberate deception — it is the ecosystem of plausible myth: content that feels true, circulates as true, and is almost impossible to verify or refute at the speed it moves.

---

## The New Conditions

Generative AI produces text, image, audio, and video that is indistinguishable from human-made content, at scale, in seconds. Algorithmic curation shows each person a version of the world calibrated to their existing beliefs. Viral circulation rewards emotional resonance over evidential quality.

The result: information literacy — always important — has become a survival skill.

---

## Two More Voices

> "One child, one teacher, one book and one pen can change the world." — Malala Yousafzai

The access problem remains. Who gets to be information-literate? Whose communities have the tools, the time, and the education to navigate this landscape? Digital literacy is not equally distributed.

> "In a world of propaganda, the answer is not counter-propaganda. The answer is education." — Eduardo Galeano

The response to misinformation is not better misinformation. It is the long, unglamorous work of building critical capacity in learners — one classroom at a time.

---

## The Teacher's Position

Teachers in 2026 operate in an information environment that did not exist when most were trained. Navigating it requires not only digital skills but epistemological honesty: the willingness to say "we don't know yet," "this requires verification," and "here is how we check."

That is not weakness. That is the intellectual integrity the situation demands.
