One of the questions posed on Day 3 was: _How much unlearning is necessary?_

The question is stronger than it first appears. It assumes that learning new approaches is not simply additive — that some things need to be set aside before new things can take root.

The practices most in need of unlearning are often the ones most deeply habituated: the instinct to fill silence, the assumption that explanation equals understanding, the reflex to simplify rather than scaffold, the habit of designing for the average learner rather than for actual learners.

Unlearning is uncomfortable in a specific way. It is not the discomfort of not knowing. It is the discomfort of suspecting that what you already know may be getting in the way.

The week in Madrid did not resolve this. It sharpened it.
