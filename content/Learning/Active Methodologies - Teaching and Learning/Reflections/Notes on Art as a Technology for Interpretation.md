Art is not the subject of a museum visit. It is the instrument.

At the [[Highlights of Spanish Art|Prado]], the mediator did not walk the group past paintings, she built an argument out of fourteen of them, curated in sequence like a text with chapters. At the [[Museo Nacional Centro de Arte Reina Sofia|Reina Sofía]], a single room reframed Guernica through African Guernica and through History Doesn't Repeat Itself, but It Does Rhyme, and the meaning of the first painting changed retroactively because of what came after it. Neither museum was teaching art history. Both were teaching how to read.

The [[Audio Guide as Scaffolding|audioguide]] made this explicit as a technique: interpretation offered in layers, available on demand, never replacing the direct encounter with the work but scaffolding the vocabulary needed to hold onto it. That is the technology part. A painting does not explain itself. It requires a method, and the museum's job is to hand visitors that method without pretending it handed them the truth.

[[Fiat Humanitas]] is where this idea was pushed furthest: six works, four artists, one thesis, an argument built entirely out of images because some conclusions cannot be reached any other way. Art, treated as a technology, stops being content to admire and becomes a lens to look through — at history, at power, at what a frame includes and excludes. The frame is never neutral. Neither is the person choosing it.
