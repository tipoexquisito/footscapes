> "The first step in the acquisition of wisdom is silence, the second listening, the third memory, the fourth practice, the fifth teaching others." — Solomon Ibn Gabirol

Healthy scepticism is not cynicism. It is the disciplined habit of asking for evidence before accepting claims — including claims that feel comfortable, that confirm existing beliefs, or that arrive from apparently authoritative sources.

---

## What It Means in Practice

Healthy scepticism means pausing before sharing. It means asking: where does this come from? Who benefits from it being believed? What would I need to see to change my mind?

It means being willing to say "I don't know yet" — and treating that as an intellectually honest position rather than a failure.

---

## Why It Matters in 2026

The information environment has changed faster than the habits required to navigate it. Generative AI, algorithmic curation, and viral circulation have made plausible misinformation easier to produce and harder to identify than at any previous point in history.

Healthy scepticism is not a personality trait. It is a teachable, learnable skill — and one of the most important things teachers can model and develop. See [[What is truth in 2026?]].
