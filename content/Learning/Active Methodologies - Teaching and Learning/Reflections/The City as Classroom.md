Madrid was never just the backdrop to the course. It was the syllabus.

A peddypaper through the streets of the centre turned a public holiday into a question: what happened here, on this square, on this balcony, on the 2nd of May 1808? The city answered before any teacher did. A plaque became a primary source. A statue became an introduction. [[Place-based Learning|Place-based learning]] does not mean taking the classroom outside; it means admitting that the street was already teaching, whether or not a lesson plan said so.

[[Toledo — The City of Three Cultures|Toledo]] pushed the same idea further. Judaism, Islam and Christianity did not illustrate coexistence there — the stone did. Synagogue, mosque and cathedral built on top of each other across centuries, without erasing what came before, made the abstract idea of layered history something you could put a hand on.

[[Casa de Campo]] and [[El Rastro]] offered a quieter version of the same lesson: a park and a market are not neutral spaces. They carry use, memory and negotiation, and a walk through either can be designed with the same intention as a classroom activity, if the teacher is willing to treat the city as a text.

None of this replaces the classroom. It relocates the question. Instead of asking what a text says, the city asks what a place has witnessed — and whether the people walking through it are still willing to notice.
