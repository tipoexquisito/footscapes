A condition in which the volume or intensity of sensory input exceeds a person's capacity to process it comfortably. Common in learners with sensory processing differences, autism spectrum conditions, ADHD, anxiety disorders, and PTSD — but experienced in milder forms by most people in certain environments.

---

## What It Looks Like

A learner experiencing sensory overload may: withdraw or become unresponsive, appear distracted or agitated, cover ears or eyes, leave the space without explanation, struggle to follow instructions, or appear to refuse participation.

These responses are not defiance. They are the nervous system's attempt to manage input it cannot process.

---

## Common Triggers in Learning Environments

Fluorescent lighting with flicker. Background noise — HVAC systems, corridor noise, multiple conversations. Strong or unfamiliar smells. Crowded or visually cluttered spaces. Unpredictable physical contact. Unexpected loud sounds.

Museum environments — with their hard floors, echoing rooms, crowds, and unfamiliar sensory conditions — can be challenging spaces for learners with sensory sensitivity.

---

## Practical Design Responses

Reducing visual clutter in classroom and display design. Providing advance notice of environmental changes. Offering a quiet or lower-stimulus space as an opt-out option. Using noise-reducing earphones or earplugs as a standard resource. Allowing movement and regulation breaks. Dimming or adjusting lighting where possible.

These adjustments cost little and benefit the learner population broadly.
