A landscape is not scenery. It is what remains after the learning has moved on.

This garden is called Footscapes because the metaphor that kept recurring across the week was never the classroom, it was the footprint: the trace a place leaves on a learner, and the trace a learner leaves on a place by paying it real attention. [[Context4Content]] gave this a name — the context does not illustrate the content, the context is the content — but the feeling came first, in Madrid, in the gap between a scheduled visit and an unscheduled one.

A learning landscape is what forms when a course stops being a sequence of sessions and becomes a terrain: overlapping, revisited, walked more than once. The same painting seen twice does not repeat, it rhymes, because the person looking at it has changed in between. The same square holds a peddypaper question and, an hour later, a completely different conversation. Nothing about the landscape moved. Everything about how it was read did.

To design for learning landscapes is to accept that the most durable lessons are rarely the ones delivered on schedule. They are the ones a place kept in reserve, waiting for a learner ready to notice them — and to trust that noticing, done properly, is itself a competence worth teaching.
