Spending a week as a learner in a foreign-language environment produced an unexpected shift: the experience of being a non-fluent speaker became a professional reference point.

When you cannot fully express yourself in the language of the room, you develop compensatory skills: you listen more attentively, you read body language more carefully, you ask for help more honestly. These are exactly the skills we want our students to develop — and we rarely think about what it costs them to use them.

[[Stephen Krashen — The Affective Filter Hypothesis]] became concrete: anxiety genuinely interferes with language acquisition. A low-stakes, supportive environment is not a courtesy. It is a pedagogical requirement.

The shift between teacher and learner roles in the course — from Portuguese-speaker to English-language participant — was a productive disorientation. Self-expression in a foreign language requires a kind of courage that rarely gets acknowledged in language classrooms.
