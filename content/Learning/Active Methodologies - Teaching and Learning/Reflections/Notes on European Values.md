The European dimension was never listed on the syllabus. It was the room itself.

Nine countries, one room, teachers speaking to teachers with no hierarchy between them. [[European Dimension in Education|The European dimension in education]] is often written down as a requirement to satisfy, a box a project must tick to qualify for funding. It felt like nothing of the sort in Madrid. It felt like the reason the funding exists in the first place.

Jean Monnet's line keeps returning in this garden because it will not stop being true: we do not unite states, we unite people. Nobody in that room arrived carrying a flag. Everybody arrived carrying a suitcase of cultural baggage, methods, assumptions, classroom habits shaped by a different education system, and the week's real curriculum was watching those differences meet without needing to resolve into sameness. [[Fiat Europa]] is where that feeling was given its full weight — Europe made not by treaty but by ruin, and remade daily, in ordinary classrooms, by ordinary practice.

That is the version of European values worth teaching forward: not a slogan, not a set of institutions, but a room where difference is the working material, and unity is something built, one shared table at a time, not something inherited by right.
