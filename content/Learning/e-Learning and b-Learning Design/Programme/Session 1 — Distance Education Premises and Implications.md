# Session 1 — Distance Education: Premises and Implications

**Date:** 5 March 2026 · **Format:** Presencial (face-to-face), 18:00–21:00 · **Facilitator:** Mário Cruz

The opening session set out the conceptual ground for the programme. It began with twenty-first-century learning competencies, critical thinking, collaboration, communication, creativity, problem-solving, and located them within a broader cultural shift toward what Joël de Rosnay calls the "pronétariat," a population that produces as much as it consumes online.

The facilitator's notes develop this thread well beyond the slide bullets: a survey of how mass media shape public opinion (subordination to the news event, hyper-personalisation of political life, the blurring of politics and entertainment, the primacy of the live over the recorded), Amaral's idea of the shift from *homo sapiens* to *homo ocular* and then to Rosnay's "homo pronetaire," and Estanque's observation that people increasingly recognise themselves in mediatised icons and scenarios. From there, the session built toward critical cultural consciousness (CCC) as something to cultivate early, so that learners can question what surrounds them and move toward emancipation in the sense of empowerment.

Critical thinking was treated as a teachable skill, with classroom strategies built around the KWL chart (Know, Want to know, Learned) and Padlet as a tool for collecting student input.

The session traced a pedagogical lineage: from explanation-based teaching toward connectivism (Siemens, Downes), pedagogy for autonomy (Jiménez Raya, Lamb), and the cycle of action, reflection, and renewed action, anchored in Dewey's definition of reflective thought and Schön's distinction between reflection *on* action and reflection *in* action. The "Creactical Skills" framework (Duarte and Cruz, 2017) was introduced as a synthesis of critical thinking, creativity, and collaborative, glocal thinking. A further note framed classrooms as mirrors of a diverse society, and teachers as empowering agents and intercultural navigators who think "glocally."

Other threads included the role of emotion in learning (Brierley; Kolb's experiential learning cycle), multimodal approaches and Universal Design for Learning, and the specific challenges the pandemic posed for higher education: engagement, autonomous study, reduced social interaction.

The session closed by mapping the core concepts that would structure the rest of the programme, e-learning, b-learning, m-learning, LMS, CMS, and the move from pedagogy to andragogy, drawing on Ausubel's meaningful learning and Alcoforado's principles of adult education.
