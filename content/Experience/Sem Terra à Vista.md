> *My little boat, / Take care, / There is no / Land in sight.*
> — Charles Simic

*Sem Terra à Vista* takes its title from the last collection of poems by the Serbian-American poet Charles Simic (*No Land in Sight*, 2022). Curated by Paula Cabaleiro, it draws a new selection from the [[Centro de Arte Oliva|Coleção Norlinda e José Lima]] around a single mood: the sense of drifting toward shipwreck with no map promising land. The curatorial text names its ground directly — war and conflict, political polarisation, disinformation and historical forgetting, migration and new discourses of hatred, the fragility of democracy, climate denial, the objectification of women's bodies, dissident identities, human rights, capitalist consumerism, the devastation of the planet. It is, in the curator's own words, a space for collective reflection that does not avoid conflict, in search of "alguma luz, de algum possível caminho: de alguma terra à vista."

The exhibition brings together a wide generational and disciplinary range: Ana Jotta, Christian Boltanski, Cindy Sherman, Paula Rego, Anish Kapoor, Helena Almeida, João Louro, Julião Sarmento and Augusto Alves da Silva among some seventy names drawn from the collection.

## Why this matters

One photograph from this exhibition carries the entire pedagogical device used in [[Experience/CAO Visit Project|CAO Visit Project]]: Augusto Alves da Silva's *3.16* (2003, Coleção Norlinda e José Lima, inv. CAO-000773), a C-print of an Azorean landscape, green and blue and close to idyllic, with the Lajes airbase barely visible in the distance. The title is a date — 16 March 2003 — the day of the Lajes Summit, when Bush, Blair, Aznar and Durão Barroso posed for the official cameras as peacemakers. Two days later, the invasion of Iraq began. Official photojournalism showed the podium and the flags. Alves da Silva photographed the silence around them.

The mediator asked the group what the exhibition's title meant before they had read a word of wall text, then returned them to the same wall an hour later and asked the same question again. The answers moved from *open sea, adrift, pirates, shipwreck* to *migration, oppression, control, no control* — the landscape unchanged, the context around it entirely different. That structure, a fixed anchor and a controlled injection of context between two askings, is described in full in [[Notes on Fiat Corde]], which tells this same afternoon from inside a conference talk rather than as a design case study. This note exists so the exhibition itself, and the artist's own frame for the work, have a place to stand independent of either telling.

As Alves da Silva has written of the series this photograph belongs to: photojournalism never produces an impartial image, because no photograph is. Nothing there was by chance, and — the pedagogical claim this visit was built to test — nothing in the framing of a classroom is either.

## Three More Stops on the Same Wall

*3.16* was the anchor, not the whole visit. Three other moments happened inside this exhibition and belong on record here.

Near [[Miguel Januário]]'s (±MaisMenos±) photographic series, the group reactivated a stencil-lettering activity designed by the museum's own mediation team: students produced their own phrase, in the ±MaisMenos± grammar of addition and subtraction, on a sheet of backdrop paper (*papel de cenário*) rather than on the gallery wall itself.

[[Ana Vieira]]'s *Sala de Espera* hangs beside a work by [[Helena Almeida]], and the pairing was read together: the political positioning of the context a work is made in against the context it is later sent into, without separating a life from its politics. It is the same claim *3.16* makes in a different key — that a work's meaning travels with the room it enters, not only the room it left.

The group stopped at [[Rigo23]]'s *MAFALDAS* (2012), the *Mujeres Autónomas Feministas Atrevidas Libres Divertidas Alegres Solidarias* tapestry, just before leaving the exhibition — art as activism, a visual grammar built for a public cause rather than a gallery wall.
