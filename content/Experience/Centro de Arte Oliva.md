The [Centro de Arte Oliva](https://centrodearteoliva.pt) occupies part of a former metalworking factory in São João da Madeira, twenty minutes' walk from [[Agrupamento de Escolas de Arrifana]]. The building did not choose art by accident. It chose it after the factory that made it stopped choosing anything.

A. J. Oliveira, Filhos & Cª opened in 1925 to supply the local hat-making industry, then diversified into farm tools, cast-iron baths and, by the 1940s, the Oliva sewing machines that gave the company its public name and its advertising life — posters by the modernist designer Alberto Cardoso, appearances in Portuguese films, campaigns run as far as the colonies in Angola and Mozambique. The buildings the museum now occupies were among the last built, from 1960, designed by architect Fernando Manuel Vieira de Campos and engineer Manuel Eduardo Coimbra de Sousa for the foundry's warehouses and general offices. At its peak the factory employed close to three thousand people. Sold in the 1970s to the American conglomerate ITT, it declined through the 1980s and closed entirely in 2010. The municipality bought part of the site and reused it, in the pattern common across post-industrial Europe, as a cultural centre rather than a ruin.

It opened in 2013 as the Núcleo de Arte da Oliva, renamed Centro de Arte Oliva in 2019 alongside a new visual identity. It is, by its own account, the only institution in Portugal working regularly and continuously with both contemporary art and outsider art (*arte bruta*) — a double focus that comes from the two private collections it holds on long-term deposit: the **Coleção de Arte Moderna e Contemporânea Norlinda e José Lima** (roughly 1,200 works by around 250 Portuguese and 250 international artists) and the **Coleção de Arte Bruta/Outsider Treger Saint Silvestre** (roughly 1,500 works by some 350 artists across Europe, Africa, the Americas and Asia). Both collections are the product of forty years of continuous private collecting, and both are shown through a rotating programme of temporary exhibitions rather than a fixed permanent display — which is why the same rooms can hold [[Horror Vacui]] one season and [[Sem Terra à Vista]] the next.

The mediation team — Daniel Costa (coordinator), Bruna Santos, Joana Ribeiro and Mariana Rocha — runs the *Visitas e Oficinas* programme that school groups like ours pass through. Daniel designs and coordinates routes with visiting teachers ahead of the visit; Bruna and Mariana are the ones who typically stand in the room and ask the questions.
