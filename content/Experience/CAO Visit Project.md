> The same wall. The same question. One hour apart. The answer had changed completely.

A module on neuromarketing — attention, emotion, framing, how images make us decide — could have stayed a slideshow. Instead, it became a visit: a class of ten at [[Centro de Arte Oliva]], in São João da Madeira, on 23 June 2026, built around three exhibitions in a single day and two mediators who knew exactly which questions to ask, and when to ask them twice.

This is the sibling piece to [[Notes on Fiat Corde]], which tells this same afternoon from inside the Fiat Corde talk — as the proof that [[Context4Content]] travels beyond the week in Madrid that gave it a name. This note tells it the other way round: as a designed learning experience, with a method, a rationale, a structure other teachers could reuse, and an honest account of the parts of the plan that did not survive contact with the day.

---

## The Group

Ten students from the Técnico de Comunicação e Serviço Digital course, 11th year, [[Agrupamento de Escolas de Arrifana]]. Roughly half are foreign-born speakers of Portuguese as an additional language; two have special educational needs. None had visited Centro de Arte Oliva before, despite it being twenty minutes' walk from the school gate.

The visit doubled as two things at once: a curricular activity for the *UFCD de Neuromarketing*, and a dissemination project for the Erasmus+ KA1 mobility the teacher had completed in Madrid the month before ([[Learning/Active Methodologies - Teaching and Learning/index|Innovative and Active Methodologies for Teaching and Learning]], May 2026). The methodologies applied on the day — [[Context4Content]], [[Place-based Learning|Place-Based Learning]], [[Cooperative Learning]], [[Think-Pair-Share]], [[Learning/Active Methodologies - Teaching and Learning/Frameworks/Reflective Practice|Reflective Practice]] — were not chosen afterwards to describe the visit. They were the methodologies experienced directly, a month earlier, in the course the visit exists to disseminate.

---

## The Design Problem

Neuromarketing, as a topic, is usually taught as content: theories of attention, case studies, mechanisms of persuasion. The risk of teaching it that way is that it stays abstract — students learn *about* framing without ever being framed themselves.

The design decision was to invert this. Rather than explain how images shape perception and then show examples, the class would be placed inside an experience where their own perception visibly shifted in real time, and only then asked to name what had happened to them, and read it back through [[Neuromap]] — the three-brains, six-stimuli, Pain-Claim-Gain-Brain framework already operational in the course. The content would arrive after the experience, not before it — [[Phenomenon-Based Learning]], the Finnish model of starting from a real phenomenon and letting the relevant disciplines cross through it, named explicitly as the frame for this visit.

That inversion required preparation that does not show up in the final product. Days before the visit, the route was walked and the questions were designed with Daniel Costa, coordinator of [[Centro de Arte Oliva|Centro de Arte Oliva's]] mediation team — an entire afternoon spent in empty galleries, deciding exactly where the group would stop and what they would be asked, before a single student arrived. *Dá trabalho.* It takes work. It is also the whole difference between a school outing and a designed learning experience. Daniel's role stopped there: he coordinated the design, but the day itself was mediated in the room by [[Bruna Santos]] and [[Mariana Rocha]], the two members of his team who actually asked the questions.

## The Day

Three exhibitions, one visit, one continuous instrument for reading them.

**Morning — [[Horror Vacui]].** Dozens of anthropomorphic figures from the Coleção Treger Saint Silvestre, Gonçalo M. Tavares's sound piece resonating through every room. Each student chose one figure and spent five minutes writing, anchored by a single question: was this work made to communicate with someone at all?

**Midday — [[Sem Terra à Vista]].** The exhibition this whole visit is remembered for, and the one with the most stops in it. A single photograph, [[Augusto Alves da Silva]]'s *3.16*, and a question asked twice an hour apart, with the mediator injecting a controlled piece of context — a *Público* clipping on the Lajes Summit — between the two askings (full account below, fuller telling in [[Notes on Fiat Corde]]). Alongside it: a stencil-lettering activity reactivated near [[Miguel Januário]]'s ±MaisMenos± series, done on backdrop paper rather than the gallery wall; a paired reading of [[Ana Vieira]]'s *Sala de Espera* and a work by [[Helena Almeida]], on the politics a work carries between the context it was made in and the context it is sent into; and a stop at [[Rigo23]]'s *MAFALDAS* tapestry just before leaving.

**Afternoon — [[Zapping]].** *Zapping: Televisão como cultura e contracultura*, seen in its final week before closing. Originally meant to be the most active part of the day, it ended up being just the guided pass through the exhibition's five núcleos, entering via Memória, with three works given particular attention: Nuno Nunes-Ferreira's compressed television archive, Rosa Baptista's fictional 1980s broadcast grid, and Antoni Muntadas's switched-off, still-transmitting *La Televisión*.

Lunch happened on site, between the second and third exhibitions, students eating what they had brought from home.

## The Method: Ask the Same Question Twice

At the entrance to *Sem Terra à Vista*, before the students could read the wall text, the mediator asked them what they thought the title meant. The answers came fast and generic: open sea, adrift, emptiness, pirates, shipwreck.

Inside, a single photograph: an Azorean landscape, green and blue, close to idyllic. While the students were still reading the image on its own terms, the mediator handed one of them a newspaper clipping to read aloud — *Público*, the same date as the photograph, reporting the Lajes summit, prime ministers awaiting Bush, still framed as diplomacy. The photograph's title was a date: 16 March 2003. Two days later, the invasion of Iraq began.

On the way out, the mediator returned the group to the same wall and asked the same question again. The answers had changed entirely: migration, oppression, control, no control. The landscape had not moved. The context around it had, and that was enough to change what the room saw.

This is the transferable part of the design: a fixed anchor (the same image, the same question), and a controlled injection of context between the two askings, so that the *change itself* becomes the lesson, rather than a claim the teacher makes about it. It is described in full, in the first person, in [[Notes on Fiat Corde]].

## What Didn't Happen, and Why That's Fine

The original design called for more than the day ended up delivering, and the honest version of this case study says so plainly.

A five-hour activation block was planned at school beforehand: warming up the three brains with archival RTP footage against a contemporary TikTok clip, a comparison exercise across the six Neuromap stimuli, an analysis of the students' own previous coursework through Pain-Claim-Gain-Brain, and mission assignments for a purpose-built disposable-camera web app, one mission per student per exhibition núcleo. Neither the activation block nor the camera app ran. The introductory class did not happen, and the app — [[The Disposable Camera]] — was not finished in time to deploy.

What ran instead was a paper observation script, briefed to the mediators beforehand and worked directly from the room. The questions the plan had designed still got asked; they were asked by Bruna Santos and Mariana Rocha, from a script, rather than triggered by an app on a student's phone. The lesson this holds for future iterations of the project: the method survives the loss of its technology. The disposable camera remains a project in development, not a project that failed — worth finishing for the next visit, not worth mourning for this one.

*Zapping* had a similar gap between plan and day. It was designed as the most active stretch of the afternoon — autonomous group work, camera missions tied to each núcleo — and what actually happened was the guided visit alone. The three highlighted works still landed; the extended, hands-on second half of the plan didn't run this time.

## Reading the Day Through Neuromap

Read back through [[Neuromap]]'s four steps, the day's three exhibitions stop being three separate museum experiences and become one continuous argument. The **Pain** each exhibition opened with was some version of not being able to trust what you're looking at — an anonymous carving, a peaceful-looking photograph, a dead television still talking. The **Claim** each made, in its own material, was that the discomfort was the point, not a failure of the object. The **Gain**, for ten students who had never set foot in the building before, was a transferable habit: ask what's missing from the frame before you trust what's in it. And the **Brain** doing the actual work, across all three rooms, was rarely the rational one — it was the instinctive read of a landscape, a figure, a screen, corrected only once the context caught up with it.

## Connections

This project sits alongside [[Transforma-te Transforma]] in [[Experience/index]] as a second case of what learning design looks like when it is built around a real encounter rather than a syllabus item. Where *Transforma-te, Transforma* demonstrates authentic learning through a student-initiated, publicly disseminated product, this project demonstrates it through curated context: the teacher's design labour is invisible in the room and entirely load-bearing.

It shares its method and its argument with [[Notes on Fiat Corde]] and with [[Context4Content]] — context is not illustration, context is the content itself. And it is the applied, classroom half of a frame whose reflective half already exists: what Madrid did to one teacher, this visit did to ten students, twenty minutes from the same school.
