> The limits of my language are the limits of my world.
> — Ludwig Wittgenstein


This philosopher, named [[Ludwig Wittgenstein]], made an observation that most people spend their lives never quite encountering.

What this means, in practice, is precise and uncomfortable. When you do not have exact words for something, you cannot think clearly about it. You cannot examine it from different angles. You cannot resolve it. You can only feel it, vaguely, repeatedly, without real resolution.

The ancient Greeks had six words for love. *Eros*, romantic desire. *Philia*, deep friendship. *Storge*, familial affection. *Pragma*, enduring mature love. *Philautia*, love of self. *Agape*, unconditional love. English has one. A person navigating a relationship problem with one word for love is working with a tool where six exist. They can feel the difference between what they want and what they have, but cannot name it. So they cannot examine it. So it stays exactly the size of the word they use to describe it.

Same feeling. More language. Completely different clarity.

---

Wittgenstein also named something he called a *language trap*. A situation in which the words themselves decide what solutions are possible.

Think of a conflict you keep returning to with someone. How do you describe it? *We never communicate. They always do this. I just feel this way.* Those words feel accurate. But Wittgenstein would say they are a trap. The moment you describe something in absolute terms, always, never, just, only, the language has already decided what the problem is and locked the mind out of seeing anything else.

He said that human problems, philosophical, personal, relational, are never truly solved. They dissolve. The moment you find precise language for what is happening, the problem does not get answered. It disappears. Because it was never as large as it felt. It was only as large as the words you had for it.

---

I have been thinking about this in relation to my own practice.

There is a version of me that spent years doing something I could not name. Designing learning experiences, building scaffolded sequences, coordinating multiple stakeholders around a shared educational outcome, facilitating reflection, creating conditions for knowledge to travel from one person to another.

I called it teaching. I called it training. I called it coordination.

The words were not wrong. But they were too small. They described the activity without naming the design intent behind it. And because the words were too small, the practice stayed too small too: contained within institutional roles, invisible to other contexts, impossible to articulate to anyone who did not already share the same frame.

*[[Learning Experience Design]]* is not a new [[Learning/Active Methodologies - Teaching and Learning/Reflections/Notes on Reflective Practice|Reflective Practice]]. It is a precise language for something that already existed.

And that precision changes everything. Not because the work changed. Because the world it can inhabit just got larger.
