# On Conclusions

> Conclusions don't look backward. They look forward.

A conclusion is not a summary. It is not a handful of sentences stacked onto the end of a piece, restating what has already been argued. If that were the function, there would be no reason to read the rest of the piece to get there.

A conclusion extends the thinking. It takes what has been built and pushes it outward: toward a bigger question, a larger stake, a next problem the argument opens rather than closes.

The anchor for this idea came from an Instagram reel, not a lecture hall or a journal article. The creator, credited as [[Jen]], was reading [[John Berger]] while preparing a [[writing workshop]], and used his essay to show what this looks like on the page. She named the source as [[The Art of Seeing]], though the Berger essay this most likely refers to is [[Ways of Seeing]] (1972). [[To verify]] before this line is cited anywhere else.

[[John Berger]]'s conclusion doesn't circle back to summarise. It moves outward, into implication: "The art of the past no longer exists as it once did." The sentence doesn't close anything. It opens a question about what happens next, about [[copyright]], about who owns the image once it circulates beyond the frame that first held it.

That is the test for a strong ending: does it look back, or does it look out?

---

## Why this matters here

This reframes how [[Notes on Fiat Corde]] and the [[Director's Cut]] should end. Not with recap. With the bigger question the piece was actually circling: who decides, who pays, who is left out of [[frame]], and what happens now that the piece is finished and the question isn't.

These three questions, [[Quem decide]], [[Quem paga]], [[Quem fica fora do frame]], are not rhetorical. They are the [[Context4Content]] thesis made visible at the level of [[argument structure]].
