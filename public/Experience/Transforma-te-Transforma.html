Transforma te, Transforma

Transforma-te, Transforma

Jul 21, 202610 min read

  • Footscapes
  • LearningExperienceDesign

An interdisciplinary project. A student assembly. A decision to make something public.

Transforma-te, Transforma is an interdisciplinary project developed at Polo 4 of Arco Maior (Carvalhido, Porto). The topic - bullying - was chosen by the students themselves in an assembly. It was not a teacher’s proposal.

Assessed through the 21CLD framework, the project reached maximum score in five of six dimensions: ICT for Learning, Collaboration, Real-World Problem-Solving, Skilled Communication and Self-Regulation.

Dissemination ran through three publications on the Arco Maior website (May and July 2025) and a presence in the June 2025 edition of the Jornal do Arco Maior — an interview with the board president conducted by a student, editorially coordinated by Filipe Gama.

The article’s central argument: the project succeeded not because of the curriculum, but in spite of usual conditions. The students acted because they were trusted with a real problem.

It is a project worth examining closely, not because it was exceptional, but because it was real.

The Origin

The project did not begin with a curriculum objective. It began with a student assembly.

In that assembly, students named bullying as something they experienced and something they wanted to address. The choice of topic, the identification of relevant experts to interview, the name of the project — all of this came from the students. The teachers’ role was to coordinate the contribution of each discipline within the pedagogical structure already in place, aligning the work with ROAD objectives and CASEL domains.

This matters. The 21st Century Learning Design framework calls this authentic learning: a problem that is real, a solution produced for a real audience, consequences that extend beyond the classroom. The students were not studying bullying. They were acting on it.

The Architecture

Eleven disciplines contributed, each with a distinct role:

  • Português wrote the interview scripts.
  • Ciências Sociais built the knowledge base by bringing the police into the classroom.
  • Marcenaria built the recording studio from OSB board.
  • Arte e Multimédia created LED neon lettering and the calligraphic posters.
  • TIC, RIC and Multimédia recorded, edited and published the podcast and produced a teaser in a single morning.
  • Matemática worked statistical literacy into the theme.
  • Inglês distinguished constructive from offensive critique.
  • Educação Física addressed self-defence techniques.
  • Cozinha and Serviço de Mesa/Bar prepared a coffee-break available during recordings.

The interdisciplinary structure was not decorative. Each discipline’s contribution was load-bearing. Remove any one of them and the product would have been diminished — or would not have existed at all.

Arte e Multimédia produced the set’s visual identity. Students designed the lettering for the LED neon “Arco Maior 4” — measuring the perimeters of calligraphic lines, working from a projected guide onto set paper with string and pins. The class visited Neónia Porto, a cultural and creative space in Porto, for inspiration. The visit was structured around the same CASEL categories used throughout the project — Observação e Atenção, Empatia e Escuta, Respeito, Reconhecer emoções and Analisar e Avaliar Situações — and assessed through a dedicated visit guide. Students were asked to photograph an artwork that provoked a strong emotional response and add it to their portfolio and, later, produced the calligraphic motivational phrases against bullying, drawn posters that would stand in the set and later circulate publicly.

Arte e Multimédia directed the podcast recording session: operating cameras, managing audio, directing the guests through the interview in a studio the school’s own students had built. But the podcast was not the only product. Students’ also produced a teaser: scripted, filmed and edited in a single morning, working from the accumulated experience of the previous sessions. That speed is the tell. The teaser was not planned. It was made possible by the confidence students had built through iteration. It is the clearest evidence of what the 21CLD framework calls self-regulation: not compliance with a task, but the capacity to mobilise what you have already learned and direct it independently.

The CASEL framework here is not a taxonomy applied after the fact. It is the architecture. S1, P1, P2 and C3 appear in the Grelha de Atividades against every single session, in every single discipline, across the full six weeks. This is what embedded social-emotional learning looks like from the inside.

The Studio

The recording set deserves its own account.

It was not rented. It was not borrowed. It was built — in a classroom, from OSB board, by the students who would sit in front of it.

Marcenaria began the work by planning, marking, calculating, cutting, assembling and fixing the panels into a standing set. Arte e Multimédia designed and installed the visual elements: the LED strips followed the calligraphic lines. The result carried both the craft of the hands that made it and the content of the minds that designed it.

When the podcast was recorded, the students were not sitting in a borrowed room. They were sitting in something they had made.

The Products

The project produced four primary outputs: two podcast episodes on YouTube, an Instagram Reel and hand-drawn motivational posters.

The podcast is the centrepiece. Two episodes were recorded, edited and published on YouTube. The guests were a CPCJ (Comissão de Proteção de Crianças e Jovens) technician who works directly with some of the students, and the school director. The interviews were conducted by the students themselves, using questions developed across five weeks of work in Português and Ciências Sociais.

A video was produced, documenting the project. It was the first product made public, linked from the May 2025 Arco Maior news item.

An Instagram Reel, published on the @arcomaiorcampanha account and embedded in the July 2025 news item, shows the calligraphic lettering created during the project. The Arco Maior description states the video was produced to put students’ “way of thinking, communicating and being to the test,” and expresses the hope that it “inspires us all to prevention and to action.” What it represents: a public act of communication, produced by young people who had been invisible, addressed to anyone who would listen.

The calligraphic posters and the LED neon lettering formed the visual environment of the studio and beyond. These were not decorations. They were acts of communication — motivational messages about bullying prevention, produced by hand, designed for impact.

Reading the Project Through 21CLD

The 21st Century Learning Design framework (Microsoft Partners in Learning, 2011) offers a useful lens for understanding what this project achieved. Across six dimensions, Transforma-te, Transforma reached the maximum score in five.

ICT for Learning: 5/5. Digital technologies were not supplementary. The podcast format required recording, editing and publication. Without ICT, the product would not have existed. The students were designers of a digital artefact for authentic users — the YouTube audience.

Collaboration: 5/5. The work was genuinely interdependent. The scriptwriters depended on the interviewers. The interviewers depended on the sound operators. The studio was built by one discipline and dressed by another. The 21CLD framework distinguishes real collaboration from group work by asking whether the product fails if any member does not fulfil their role. It would have.

Knowledge Construction: 4/5. Students researched, synthesised and applied knowledge about bullying across disciplines and in a new, real-world context. The score falls short of maximum not because the interdisciplinarity was weak, but because it was not systematically assessed as a formal learning objective across all contributing disciplines.

Real-World Problem-Solving: 4/4. Maximum. Bullying was a real problem, identified by the students themselves, with direct impact on their community. The solutions were implemented and communicated to a real, external audience.

Skilled Communication: 4/4. Maximum. The podcast is multimodal — audio, video, photography, lettering, live interview. It was conceived for a specific audience with a specific communicative intent. Expert testimony was included as supporting evidence.

Self-Regulation: 4/4. Maximum. The ROAD structure meant students had individual learning objectives and success criteria defined in advance. They planned their work, distributed roles and iterated. The project closed on 20 February with a formal self-assessment followed by the pedagogical team’s final ROAD evaluation — a dual-authored record of what each student had actually done.

What 21CLD reveals here is not a curriculum design. It reveals what happened when students were trusted with a real problem.

Dissemination

The project was disseminated through three public channels.

The Arco Maior website published two news items directly tied to this project. The first documents the project and links to both the podcast and the student-produced video. The school’s framing is explicit: the students “learned to make Arco Maior a safer, more empathetic and respectful place for everyone.”

The second embeds an Instagram Reel from the @arcomaiorcampanha campaign account — a public channel connected to Polo 4 Carvalhido. The Reel shows the calligraphic lettering created during the project.

A third item documents an interview with Prof. Joaquim Azevedo - founder of Arco Maior - conducted by student Mara as part of the preparation for the June 2025 edition of the Jornal do Arco Maior. That edition, coordinated editorially by Filipe Gama and identified by ISSN, addressed the theme of education and society in 2050. The student interviewing a board president about the future of education is not incidental to the project. It is the project’s logic made visible: young people who started by examining bullying in their own school ended by questioning what education should become.

Connections

This project sits at the intersection of several threads that run through index and beyond.

It demonstrates Project-Based Learning at a level that the 21CLD framework recognises as maximum performance: student-initiated, interdisciplinary, anchored in a real problem, producing artefacts for a real public. It demonstrates CASEL competences not as items on a checklist but as lived practice — the students who ran the podcast were, in the very act of running it, practising self-regulation, empathy and responsible decision-making.

It also demonstrates something that frameworks do not always name clearly: the conditions under which young people — including those in highly vulnerable situations — produce work of genuine quality. Those conditions are not primarily technological or curricular. They are relational. The students acted because they trusted the institution. The institution trusted them back.

The ROAD framework, the CASEL referential and the pedagogical model of Arco Maior did not produce the project. They made it possible for the project to produce itself.
