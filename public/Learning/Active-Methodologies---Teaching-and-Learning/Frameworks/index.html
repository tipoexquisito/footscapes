Educational Frameworks

John Dewey

“If we teach today as we taught yesterday, we rob our children of tomorrow.”

The frameworks in this garden are not a curriculum. They are lenses. Each one was introduced during the course and then tested against real contexts: a classroom in Madrid, a museum in the Prado, the streets of Toledo.

Some of these frameworks you already use without naming them. That’s the point. Naming them gives you a handle. A handle gives you a way to share them with others.

Active Methodologies

Learning as something you do, not something that happens to you.

Active Methodologies is the umbrella. The course didn’t introduce these as theory. It enacted them, session after session, so you experienced them as a learner before reflecting on them as an educator.

Structures for cooperative work included Think-Pair-Share, Jigsaw, Let’s Have a Date, Pass the Page, Shared Reading, Team Interview and Numbered Heads Together. Forming Cooperative Groups was its own conversation: random, teacher-selected or self-selected, each with implications for equity and dynamics. Some appear multiple times, each instance as a methodology in action rather than a description of one.

Also worth noting: Freewrite, Priority Diamond and Quiz-Quiz-Trade as lightweight structures for reflection, critical thinking and review.

Cooperative Learning

Cooperative Learning rests on five pillars: positive interdependence, individual and group accountability, promotive interaction, social skills and group processing.

The theoretical anchors include Vygotsky’s Vygotsky — Zone of Proximal Development, Ausubel’s Meaningful Learning, Rogers’s Experiential Learning and Krashen’s Stephen Krashen — The Affective Filter Hypothesis. Gardner’s Howard Gardner — Multiple Intelligences also appeared here, as a reminder that learners are not one thing.

Inclusive and Differentiated Learning

Designing for everyone, not adjusting for some.

Universal Design for Learning starts from one assumption: every learner is highly variable. The three principles of UDL, multiple means of engagement, representation and expression, were explored through a film, group discussion and a direct comparison between traditional and UDL classroom activities.

Related frameworks: CLIL, Scaffolding and Multiple Means of Representation. On the CLIL side, BICS and CALP surfaced as one of the more practical distinctions of the week, alongside Expressing Opinions using PREP as a scaffolding structure for expressing and organising opinions.

Technology and Digital Competences

Which door are you trying to open? Which key do you need?

The The SAMR Model (Substitution, Augmentation, Modification, Redefinition) framed how technology integration is a spectrum, not a checklist. DigCompEdu, the European Digital Competence Framework for Educators, and the SELFIE Tool for Teachers provided a self-assessment lens.

AI Literacy was positioned as distinct from digital literacy, drawing on UNESCO, OECD and the EU AI Act frameworks, with a horizon marker: PISA 2029 will assess media and AI literacy.

Tools explored in practice: Mentimeter, NotebookLM, Curipod, Padlet Arcade, Genially, Webwise. Not as mandatory tools to adopt, but as examples of SAMR levels in action.

Literacies

Reading the world, not just the word.

Visual Literacy, AI Literacy and Media Literacy belong together. The Prado made visual literacy concrete in ways no slide could. A Goya puzzle, a fragmented narrative, a painting reassembled before visiting the original. The question “what can you see, what do you think is happening?” is both a CLIL strategy and a visual literacy practice.

Experiential Learning

The body learns too.

Place-based Learning, Sensory Learning, Embodied Learning and Mindfulness share a thread: context is not a backdrop. It is part of the learning itself.

Tableau appeared as a theatre technique for embodied storytelling. The marzapan tasting was a mindfulness exercise and a history lesson at the same time. The 5-4-3-2-1 grounding technique and beach ball breathing were introduced as practical tools for sensory regulation, with a direct note on Sensory overload and the learners for whom sensory richness is not an asset.

Toledo was not a field trip. It was a methodology.

Still finding their place

Dissemination and Notes on European Dimension resist easy categorisation. They are frameworks for what happens after learning and for the larger context that makes this kind of learning possible. They will find their place.
